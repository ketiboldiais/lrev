To prevail on some legal claim, the claimant's attorney must craft arguments consisting of two core ingredients: (1) the law, and (2) the facts. Unlike the law, not every fact or purported fact can be presented in court. This is because of the body of law called **evidence**.

The law of evidence governs how testimony, writing, tangibles, and other objects presented to the senses are offered in trial to prove the existence or non-existence of a fact.

This outline focuses on the Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE) and notable deviations among the states (particularly California).

## Categories of Evidence
- The FRE divides evidence into six categories:
	1. oral testimony - a witness takes the stand and answers the attorneys' questions
	2. real evidence - physical evidence claimed to have played a direct role in the case
	3. documents - writings or recordings of information
	4. demonstrative evidence - charts, tables, pictures, maps, graphs, or reenactments (demos) to demonstrate some fact
	5. stipulations - facts that both parties agree are true for litigation purposes
	6. judicial notice - facts that the court recognizes as indisputably true
	7. circumstantial evidence - evidence where the jury must make an inference connecting the evidence to a disputed fact

- Evidence must be relevant to be admissible.
- Evidence is relevant if it's both:
	1. [ ] [probative](#probative), <AND/>
	2. [ ] [material](#material)

### Probative
- Evidence is probative if it has **any tendency** to make a fact more or less probative that it would be without the evidence.

### Material
- Evidence is material if the fact it supports impacts how the action's outcome is determined.

## Exclusion for Relevance
Even if evidence is relevant, courts can exclude the evidence because of (1) legal relevance, or (2) public policy.

### Exclusion: Legal Relevance
- A court can exclude relevant evidence if its probative value is **substantially outweighed** by a danger of one or more of the following:
	1. [ ] <b>u</b>nfair <b>p</b>rejudice
	2. [ ] <b>co</b>nfusing the issues
	3. [ ] <b>m</b>isleading the jury  
  4. [ ] undue <b>d</b>elay
	5. [ ] <b>wa</b>sting time, <OR/>
	6. [ ] needlessly <b>p</b>resenting cumulative evidence
- Mnemonic: UPCOMDWAP

### Exclusion: Public Policy
- Even if evidence is relevant or admissible under other evidentiary rules, courts can exclude the evidence for public policy reasons.

### Subsequent Remedial Measures
From a policy perspective, we want individuals or firms to fix a problem when they see it. Accordingly, there are rules on whether a "fix" can be admitted into evidence.
- A **subsequent remedial measure** is an action taken after an injury or harm to prevent future injuries.
- For example, after ${A}$ falls down a flight of stairs at amusement park ${x,}$  ${x}$ installs a handrail. That installation is a subsequent remedial measure.
- Subsequent remedial measures are **NOT**  admissible to prove:
	1. [negligence](/torts/main#negligence),
	2. [defective products](/torts/main#defective-products),
	3. [defective product design](/torts/main#defective-in-design), <OR/>
	3. culpable conduct
- But, evidence of subsequent remedial measures are admissible for:
	1. showing agency,
	2. showing ownership of property,
	3. showing control of property, <OR/>
	4. impeachment purposes

### Compromise Offers & Settlement Negotiations
We want individuals or firms to compromise or settle — it gives the courts more time and resources to focus on other claims. Thus, there are rules on whether an offer to compromise or settle can be admitted.

- If:
	1. [ ] the evidence to be admitted is:
		- [ ] an offer, conduct, or statement
		- [ ] made during negotiations
		- [ ] to settle or compromise
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] the evidence's purpose is to:
		- prove a disputed claim,
		- prove an amount, <OR/>
		- impeach a witness
  - => the evidence is INADMISSIBLE.

### Offers to Pay Medical Expenses
Given the state of medical insurance in the United States and the potential complexity of calculating damages, we want individuals or firms to offer paying medical expenses. Accordingly, there are rules on whether an offer to pay a medical expense is admissible.

- If:
	1. [ ] the evidence is an offer to pay a medical expense, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the evidence's purpose is to prove liability for the plaintiff's injuries,
  - => the evidence is INADMISSIBLE.

### Guilty Pleas
- The following are inadmissible evidence **against the defendant**:
	1. [ ] withdrawn guilty pleas
	2. [ ] no contest pleas
	3. [ ] statements made while negotiating with prosecutors, <AND/>
	4. [ ] statements made during plea negotiations
- However, the pleas and statements made during negotiations are admissible if:
	1. [ ] fairness requires admission, <OR/>
	2. [ ] the forum is a perjury hearing

### Liability Insurance
- If:
	1. [ ] the evidence shows that:
		- [ ] a person was insured against liability, <OR/>
		- [ ] a person was not insured against liability,
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] the evidence's purpose is to
		- [ ] prove that the person acted negligently, <OR/>
		- [ ] prove that the person acted wrongfully
  - => the evidence is admissible
- For all other purposes, this exclusionary rule does not apply.
- Common examples of such purposes:
  - proving a witness's bias
  - proving a witness's prejudice
  - proving agency
  - proving ownership of property
  - proving control of property

## Character Evidence
- Character evidence is evidence of a person's character or a person's specific character trait.
- E.g., that a person is violent, honest, deceitful, lazy, etc.
- In trial, there are three common forms of character evidence:
	1. [reputation testimony](#reputation-testimony)
	2. [opinion testimony](#opinion-testimony)
	3. [specific instances](#specific-instances)
- Character evidence is often used to paint a person a particular way, so as to cast doubt on claims. There are limitations on whether character evidence is admissible.

### Reputation Testimony
- Reputation testimony is testimony about the subject's reputation in the community.
- E.g., "Everyone in town knows alderman ${B}$ takes bribes."

### Opinion Testimony
- Opinion testimony is testimony from ${A}$ about ${B.}$
- E.g., ${A}$ testifies, "I personally know ${B}$ is easily angered."

### Specific Instances
- A specific instance is testimony from ${A}$ about a specific time where ${B}$ acted a certain way or perform a certain act.
- E.g., ${A}$ testifies, "I saw ${B}$ cuss out his wife."

### Character Evidence in Civil Cases
- In civil cases, if:
	1. [ ] the character evidence's purpose is to
		- [ ] show that a person
		- [ ] has a propensity to act according to an alleged character trait
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] neither of these conditions are met:
		- [ ] the alleged character trait is an essential element of a claim or defense
		<OR/>
		- [ ] the case is based on the defendant's sexual misconduct,
  - => the character evidence is inadmissible
- The rule above means that character evidence is generally inadmissible in a civil case unless it falls under one of the two exceptions:
  - the character trait is an essential claim or defense (e.g., in  defamation, negligent hiring, negligent entrustment, child custody cases), or
  - the case is based defendant's sexual misconduct (to introduce past sexual assault or child molestation by the defendant)

### Criminal Cases
- If:
	1. [ ] the proponent is the State
	2. [ ] the character evidence is of the defendant's bad character
	3. [ ] the purpose of the evidence is to:
		- [ ] prove that the defendant has a propensity to have committed the crime
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the defendant has not [opened the door to bad character evidence](#opening-the-door-to-bad-character-evidence),
  - => the character evidence is inadmissible.
- If the State is permitted to introduce bad character evidence, it can only introduce the evidence to rebut the defendant's positive character evidence.
- The State may do so by:
	1. calling its own character witness,
		- If the State takes this route, the witness can only give reputation or opinion testimony (NOT specific instances of conduct)
	<OR/>
	2. cross-examining the defendant's character witness
		- If the State takes this route, it can introduce evidence of specific instances as long as they relate to the same character trait in question.

#### Opening the Door to Bad Character Evidence
- A defendant opens the door to bad character evidence if:
	1. [ ] the defendant presents evidence of their positive character,
	2. [ ] the evdience is pertinent to the crime charged, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the evidence is presented through reputation or opinion testimony (NOT specific instances of conduct)

#### Evidence of the Victim's Character
- A criminal defendant can introduce reputation or opinion testimony of the victim's character only if:
  - [ ] the victim's character is relevant to
  - [ ] at least one of the defenses asserted
- If the defendant introduces such evidence, the State can rebut the evidence by:
	1. presenting evidence that the defendant possesses the same character trait,
	2. the victim possesses a relevant positive character trait
	<OR/>
	3. If the rebuttal is done during cross examination, presenting specific instances of conduct.

### Specific Instances of Conduct
- IF:
	1. [ ] the specific instance of conduct's purpose is to show:
		- [ ] motive or opportunity,
		- [ ] intent,
		- [ ] absence of mistake,
		- [ ] identity, <OR/>
		- [ ] common plan or preparation
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] there is sufficient evidence to support a jury finding that
		- [ ] the defendant committed the prior act,
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] the probative value of the specific instance of conduct is NOT substantially ouweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice to the jury,
  - => the specific instance of conduct is ADMISSIBLE
- ELSE:
  - => the specific instance of conduct is INADMISSIBLE

### Habit & Routine Practices
- IF:
	1. [ ] the evidence is of
		- [ ] a person's habit, <OR/>
		- [ ] an organization's routine practice
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] the evidence's purpose is to prove that
		- [ ] the person or organization
		- [ ] acted according to the habit or routine practice
  - => the evidence is ADMISSIBLE
- Note: As long as the conditions above are met, courts can admit the evidence regardless of whether it's corrobrated.

## Impeaching Witnesses
In trial, attorneys will try to discredit witnesses through impeachment. The most common ways to do so are:

1. [Attacking the witness's character for truthfulness](#character-for-truthfulness),
	- Essentially communicating to the jury: "They've lied before, who's to say they aren't lying now?"
2. [Presenting evidence of a witness's prior convictions](#prior-convictions)
	- "If they've done this crime, lying is a no brainer."
3. [Presenting evidence of prior inconsistent statements](#prior-inconsistent-statements)
	- "They said this last time, and now they're saying this. Which is it?"
4. Presenting evidence questioning the witness's sensory competence
5. Attacking the witness's hearsay statements

### Character for Truthfulness
- A witness's credibility can be attacked by introducing [character evidence](#character-evidence) of the witness's untruthfullness.
- That character evidence can be introduced only through [reputation testimony](#reputation-testimony) or [opinion testimony](#opinion-testimony).
- A witness's credibiltiy **cannot be bolstered**.
  - This means that evidence of a truthful character can only be introduced to defend against an attack on truthfulness.

#### Character for Truthfulness on Cross-examination
- On cross, a witness's credibility can be attaced with [specific instances of conduct](#specific-instances) only if:
	1. [ ] the specific instance regardes the witness's truthfullness,
	2. [ ] there is a good faith belief in the prior misconduct,
	3. [ ] the specific instance does NOT involve an arrest,
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the specific instance does NOT involve the consequences of an arrest

#### Extrinsic Evidence of Specific Instaces
- Generally, extrinsic evidence of specific instances of conduct are INADMISSIBLE.
- Example:
  - Witness ${A}$ lied on a job application.
  - On cross, opposing counsel can ask ${A}$ about the job application.
  - But, the application itself cannot be introduced as extrinsic evidence.

#### Prior Convictions
Evidence of a prior conviction can be a powerful tool in painting a witness a particular way. Whether such evidence is admissible depends on what the convicition was for, and how much time has passed since the conviction.

##### Convictions Involving Dishonesty
- If:
	1. [ ] the prior conviction is for a felony or misdemeanor,
	2. [ ] the conviction involves dishonesty, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the conviction falls within the [10-year restriction](#10-year-restriction)
	4. [ ] the conviction was not pardoned
	5. [ ] the conviction was not annulled
	6. [ ] the convicted was not later found innocent
	<AND/>
	7. [ ] the purpose of the prior conviction is to attack the witness's credibility
  - => the prior conviction is ADMISSIBLE
	- => if the 10-year restriction is not satisfied, the proponent must satisfy the [reverse legal relevance test](#reverse-legal-relevance-test) for the evidence to be admissible.

##### Prior Felonies
- If:
	1. [ ] the prior conviction is for a crime that:
		- is punishable by death, <OR/>
		- is punishable by more than 1 year imprisonment
	2. [ ] the conviction does not involve dishonesty,
	3. [ ] the conviction is [legally relevant](#legally-relevant-conviction)
	4. [ ] the conviction falls within the [10-year restriction](#10-year-restriction)
	5. [ ] the conviction was not pardoned
	6. [ ] the conviction was not annulled
	7. [ ] the convicted was not later found innocent
	<AND/>
	8. [ ] the purpose of the prior conviction is to attack the witness's credibility
	- => the prior conviction is ADMISSIBLE
  - => if the 10-year restriction is not satisfied, the proponent must satisfy the [reverse legal relevance test](#reverse-legal-relevance-test) for the to be admissible.

##### 10-year Restriction
- A conviction falls within the 10-year restriction only if:
  - [ ] less than 10 years have passed since the later of:
    - [ ] the date of conviction, <OR/>
    - [ ] the date of release
- Example 1:
  - ${A}$ is convicted for burglary in Jan. 1, 2000, and released in Jan. 1, 2004.
  - The later of these two dates is Jan. 1, 2004.
  - Thus, the latest the evidence of the burglary conviction can be admitted as evidence is Dec. 31, 2013.
- Example 2:
  - ${A}$ is convicted for fraud in Jan. 1, 2000.
  - But, ${A}$ does not serve time.
  - Thus, there is no release date, so the applicable start date is the date of conviction, Jan. 1, 2000.
  - The latest the evidence of the fraud conviction can be admitted as evidence is Dec. 31, 2009.

##### Legally Relevant Conviction
- A conviction is legally relevant if its probative value is not substantially outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice.

### Prior Inconsistent Statements
- A witness's credibility can be attacked by introducing the witness's prior inconsistent statements only if:
	- [ ] the prior inconsistent statement's purpose is to
	- [ ] attack the witness' credibility on a [relevant matter](#collateral-matters).
- A prior inconsistent statement is a statement where, at an earlier time, the witness said ${x,}$ but is now saying ${y.}$
- Thus, prior inconsistent statements always have two parts:
	1. the first statement ${P_s}$, and
	2. the second statement ${Q_s}$
- These statement parts can be made in court or outside of court.
- Thus, there are four combinations of prior inconsistent statements:
	1. ${P_s}$ made in court and ${Q_s}$ made in court — ${[P_s, Q_s].}$
	2. ${P_s}$ made outside of court and ${Q_s}$ made outside of court — ${(P_s, Q_s).}$
	3. ${P_s}$ made in court and ${Q_s}$ made outside of court — ${[P_s, Q_s).}$
	4. ${P_s}$ made outside of court and ${Q_s}$ made in court — ${(P_s, Q_s].}$

### Sensory Competence
- A witness's credibility can be attacked by showing that the witness has a deficiency in any of the following:
	1. [ ] their ability to perceive information,
	2. [ ] their ability to recall information, <OR/>
	3. [ ] their ability to relate information
	4. [ ] provided that:
		- [ ] the information is [not collateral](#collateral-matters).

### Attacking & Supporting the Declarant
- If:
	1. [ ] a hearsay statement has been admitted in evidence,
	2. [ ] there is evidence that
		- [ ] if the hearsay's declarant had testified as a witness,
		- [ ] the evidence would be admissible
	- => the hearsay declarant's credibility and be attacked or supported with such evidence
- The court can admit evidence of the declarant's inconsistent statement or conduct, regardless of when it occurred or whether the declarant had an opportunity to explain or deny the statement.
- If the party the hearsay statement attacks calls the declarant as a witness, that party can question the declarant about the statement on cross-examination.

### Collateral Matters
- A matter is relevant only if it is not a **collateral matter**.
- A collateral matter is a matter unrelated to the issue in controversy.

## Reverse Legal Relevance Test
- The reverse legal relevance test is satisfied only if:
	1. [ ] the evidence's proponent shows
	2. [ ] that the evidence's probative value substantially outweighs the danger of unfair prejudice

## Opinion Testimony
Opinion testimony consists of opinions given by witnesses. Evidence law disfavors opinion testimony. Ideally, witnesses should only tell the court what they _know_, rather than what they _think_. Accordingly, the FRE bends slighly towards restricting opinion testimony.

### Lay Witnesses
- A lay witness is any person that:
	1. [ ] gives an opinion, and
	2. [ ] is not expert
- Lay witnesses are presumed competent unless proven otherwise.
- Thus, if party ${A}$ wants to disqualify witness ${W}$ as incompetent, ${A}$ bears the burden of proving that ${W}$ is incompetent.
- In determining whether ${A}$ has met this burden, courts consider the weight of the evidence ${A}$ presents, rather than whether the evidence ${A}$ presents is admissible.

#### What Lay Witnesses Can Give Opinions On
- Lay witnesses can only give an opinion that:
	1. [ ] is rationally based on the witness's perception,
	2. [ ] is helpful to
		- [ ] clearly understanding the witness's testimony, <OR/>
		- [ ] determining the fact in issue,
	3. [ ] does NOT require scientific knowledge that only an expert witness can provide,
	4. [ ] does NOT require technical knowledge that only an expert witness can provide,
	<AND/>
	5. [ ] does NOT require specialized knowledge that only an expert witness can provide

### Expert Witnesses
- An expert witness is any person that:
	- [ ] is qualified as an expert witness by the Court
- Expert witnesses can only give an opinion if the following requirements are met:
	1. [ ] the expert witness is qualified by possessing sufficient knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education,
	2. [ ] the expert's scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge
		- [ ] helps the trier of fact understand the evidence, <OR/>
		- [ ] helps the trier of fact determine a fact in issue
	3. [ ] the opinion is based on sufficient facts or data,
	4. [ ] the opinion is formed from reliable principles and methods,
	<AND/>
	5. [ ] the expert witness has reliably applied their field's principles and methods to the case's facts

#### Ultimate Issues
- Expert witness **cannot** give opinions on ultimate issues.
- An ultimate issue is any issue that only the jury can decide.
- But, they can give an opinion on whether a criminal defendant had the requisite mental state for the crime charged.

### Obtaining Opinion Testimony
Often, counsel will want to get certain responses from a witness. Evidence law imposes rules on when and how attorneys can get witnesses to talk.

#### Spousal Immunity Privilege (SIP)
- IF:
  1. [ ] a witness ${A}$ is called
	2. [ ] to testify against a person ${B}$
	3. [ ] in either of:
		- [ ] a criminal proceeding, <OR/>
		- [ ] a grand jury proceeding
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] ${A}$ and ${B}$ are in a valid marriage
	- => ${A}$ has a right under federal law to refuse to testify
- In federal court and for most states, the witness-spouse (${A}$) holds the privilege and can choose whether or not to testify.
- In a minority of states, the defendant-spouse (${B}$) holds the privilege and can prevent the witness-spouse from testifying.

#### Confidential Marital Communications Privilege (CMCP)
- A communication between spouses is privileged if:
	1. [ ] the communication was made during their valid marriage, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the communication was intended to be confidential (e.g., "pillow talk")
- CMCP is a separate avenue for preventing a person from testifying against their spouse.
- In contrast to SIP, CMCP:
	- can be asserted by either spouse,
	- applies to criminal _and_ civil proceedings, and
	- extends beyond the end of the marriage
- Needless to say, CMCP is commonly asserted in divorce proceedings.

#### Attorney-Client Privilege (ACP)
- For a communication to be protected under ACP, the following must satisfy the following conditions:
	1. [ ] the communication is between one of the following:
		- [ ] an attorney and a client,
		- [ ] an attorney and a prospective client,
	2. [ ] the communication's purpose was to further legal services,
	3. [ ] the communication was intended to be confidential
	4. [ ] there are no [corporate client issues](#acp--corporate-clients)
	<AND/>
	5. [ ] [ACP's exceptions do not apply](#acp-exceptions)

##### ACP & Corporate Clients
- For some states, if the client is a corporation, ACP only applies to communications between the attorney and the corporation's _control group members._
	- E.g., board directors and executive officers
- Under federal law, ACP extends to non-control group members iff:
	- [ ] the communication is to seek legal advice
	- [ ] _for the corporation_

##### ACP Exceptions
- If any of these conditions are true, ACP _does not apply_:
	1. [ ] the communication's purpose was to:
		- [ ] further legal services <AND/>
		- [ ] the legal services were to further a crime or fraud
	2. [ ] there is litigation on a dispute between the attorney and client
	3. [ ] the communication is:
		- [ ] between the attorney and co-clients, <AND/>
		- [ ] the co-clients are now adverse parties
		- [ ] in a civil proceeding
	4. [ ] the client has [waived the privilege](#acp-waivers)

##### ACP Waivers
- ACP is waived iff the client:
	1. [ ] [voluntary waives the privilege](#voluntary-waivers), <OR/>
	2. [ ] [involuntary waives the privilege](#involuntary-waivers)

###### Voluntary Waivers
Because ACP is a privilege held by the client (_not_ the attorney), the client can waive the privilege intentionally.

- If the client
	1. [ ] verbally waives the privilege in court, or
	2. [ ] intentionally shares the communication with a third party (called an **intentional disclosure**)
	- => ACP is waived

###### Involuntary Waivers
An involuntary waiver occurs if the client(s) accidentally disclose the communication to a third party. E.g., accidentally forwarding their attorney's email to another. This is called an **inadvertent disclosure**.

- If the privilege holder
	- [ ] inadvertently discloses the communication,
	- [ ] with a third party,
	<AND/>
	- [ ] the privilege holder took reasonable steps
	- [ ] to prevent and rectify the disclosure
	- => ACP is NOT waived
	- ELSE:
		- => ACP is waived

## Tangible Evidence
- Tangible evidence is any evidence that:
	- [ ] is not oral or verbal testimony
- If a piece of evidence qualifies as documentary evidence, it MUST be authenticated.
- Generally, there are four categories of documentary evidence:
	1. physical evidence,
	2. documentary evidence,
	3. self-authenticating evidence, and
	4. oral recordings
- A mnemonic: PHYDOSER

### Physical Evidence
- Physical evidence includes items like weapons, clothing, jewelry — the usual, daily tangible items.
- Physical evidence is authenticated iff:
	1. [ ] the proponent shows that
	2. [ ] the physical evidence is what the proponent claims it to be
	3. [ ] by calling a witness to testify that:
		- [ ] the physical evidence is what the proponent claims it to be
		<OR/>
		- [ ] the physical evidence's chain of custody is unbroken
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the witness's testimony is based on their personal knowledge of the physical evidence

### Documentary Evidence
- Documentary evidence consists of letters, contracts, handwritten notes, emails, text messages, and so on. Essentially, textual evidence.
- Documentary evidence is authenticated iff at least one of the following conditions is met:
	1. [ ] all parties stipulate (agree) that the evidence is authentic
	2. [ ] the proponent shows that the evidence is what they claim it to be through witness testimony,
	3. [ ] if the evidence is written by hand, through handwriting verification by either:
		- [ ] an express witness comparing the writing in question with another writing that has been proven to be genuine,
		- [ ] a trier of fact comparing the writing in question with another writing that has been proven to be genuine,
		- [ ] a non-expert witness ONLY IF:
			- [ ] the witness has personal knowledge of the handwriting in question, <AND/>
			- [ ] the witness did not obtain that personal knowledge as preparation for litigation
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the [best evidence rule](#best-evidence-rule) is not violated

### Self-authenticating Evidence
Some evidence is so obviously authentic that they do not require extrinsic proof. Such evidence is called self-authenticating evidence.

- If the tangible evidence is any of the following:
	1. [ ] a public document bearing a seal,
	2. [ ] a certified copy of a public record,
	3. [ ] an official publication issued by a public authority,
	4. [ ] a newspaper,
	5. [ ] a periodical (e.g., an academic journal or magazine),
	6. [ ] a notarized document,
	7. [ ] commercial paper,
	8. [ ] a trade inscription (a sign, tag, or label indicating origin, ownership, or control; e.g., clothing tags), <OR/>
	9. [ ] a record:
		1. [ ] of a regulary conducted business activity
		2. [ ] certified by a custodian of the record
	- => the evidence is authentic

### Oral Recordings
Oral recordings form a separate category because of the U.S.'s history with wiretapping and concealed recording devices.
- An oral recording is authenticated iff:
	1. [ ] the proponent shows that
	2. [ ] the voice in the recording is who the proponent claims it to be
	3. [ ] by calling a witness to testify
	4. [ ] the witness has heard the person's voice through any means
	<AND/>
	5. [ ] the [best evidence rule](#best-evidence-rule) is not violated

## Best Evidence Rule
- IF:
	1. [ ] the evidence is:
		1. [ ] [documentary evidence](#documentary-evidence), <OR/>
		2. [ ] an [oral recording](#oral-recordings)
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] one of the following issues exists:
		1. [ ] the contents of the evidence are at issue, <OR/>
		2. [ ] a witness is relying on the document when testifying
	- THEN the evidence's proponent must:
		1. [ ] provide the original document (i.e., the original evidence) <OR/>
		2. [ ] an accurate duplicate
	- ELSE:
		- the best evidence rule does not apply; the rule is not violated

- Common pieces of evidence that implicate the rule:
	- writings,
	- recordings,
	- photographs,
	- electronic documents,
	- x-rays,
	- videos

## Hearsay
Hearsay is a subset of oral testimony. In short, it's a subset of statements that take the form:

$$
	A:~~``B~~\text{said}~~x"
$$

where ${A}$ is the speaker, ${B}$ is another person, and ${x}$ is a statement. Why are these statements problematic? Because they're statements about a statement being made. As such, hearsay statements present two possible truth sets:

The first truth set maps to ${P_s,}$ and the second truth set maps to ${Q_s.}$ In other words, for all hearsay statements, there are two possible things the hearsay statement's proponent is trying to do:

1. Asserting that ${P_s}$ is true (that ${B}$ did, in fact, say ${x}$), OR
2. Asserting that ${Q_s}$ is true (that ${B}$'s statement ${x}$ is true)

The problematic portion — and what implicates hearsay — is ${Q_s.}$ In legalese, ${Q_s}$ is called the **matter asserted**. The core challenge in dealing with hearsay is figuring out what exactly ${P_s}$ is trying to prove. If it's being offered to prove ${Q_s,}$ then we say that the statement is offered _to prove the truth of the matter asserted._ If ${P_s}$ was made out of court and is now being offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted, we're in hearsay territory.

If, however, ${P_s}$ is being offered to prove that ${B}$ did in fact say ${x,}$ rather than that ${Q_s}$ is true, then the statement is not considered hearsay. Additionally, if ${P_s}$ was made in court, rather than outside of court, then the statement is also not considered hearsay.

### Definition of Hearsay
- A statement is considered hearsay if and only if:
	1. [ ] the statement (${P_s}$) is made out of court
	2. [ ] the statement is offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted (${Q_s}$), and
	4. [ ] the statement is not [explicitly listed as non-hearsay](#explicit-non-hearsay), <AND/>
	3. [ ] the statement does not fall under an [exception](#hearsay-exceptions)
- If a statement is considered hearsay, it is inadmissible.

### What Counts as a Statement
- Evidence that is not considered a statement is not hearsay.
- Statements include:
	1. a person's oral assertions,
	2. a person's written assertions,
	3. if the person intended it as an assertion, a person's nonverbal conduct
		- e.g., a head node, thumbs-up, etc.
	4. silence (_see_ [Adoptive Admissions](#adoptive-admissions))
	5. statements made by an agent (_see_ [Vicarious Admissions](#vicarious-admissions))

### Non-hearsay
- Statements that do not satisfy the prongs of hearsay's definition are not considered hearsay and are admissible. There are several ways for a statement ${P_s}$ to be considered non-hearsay:

1. The statement is not made out of court,
2. The statement is not being offered to prove ${Q_s,}$
3. The statement is explicitly non-hearsay

#### Statements Not Offered to Prove Truth of the Matter Asserted
- Statements that are not offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted (checkbox 2 in the definition) are not considered hearsay and are admissble.
- Common examples of such statements:
	1. verbal acts of independent legal significance
		- essentially, the statement is being offered to prove that the statement was made; not to prove that the statement made was true
	2. statements offered to show the effect on the listener
	3. statements offered to show the declarant's mental state or state of mind,
	4. statements offered for [impeachment purposes](#impeaching-witnesses)

#### Explicit Non-hearsay
- Even if all the other prongs of hearsay are met, the statement might fail condition 4: statements explicitly listed as non-hearsay.
- Under the FRE, these statements include the following:
	1. prior inconsistent statements
	2. prior consistent statement
	4. prior statements of indentification
	5. admissions by a party opponent

##### Explicit Non-hearsay: Prior Inconsistent Statements
- A statement is not considered hearsay if:
	1. [ ] the proponent is admitting the statement for substantive purposes,
	2. [ ] the declarant is testifying at trial and subject to cross-examination,
	3. [ ] the statements were previously made under penalty of perjury,
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the statement is a prior statement inconsistent with present testimony being given at trial

##### Explicit Non-hearsay: Prior Consistent Statements
- A statement is not considered hearsay if:
	1. [ ] the proponent is admitting the statement to rebut a claim that
	2. [ ] the declarant either:
		1. [ ] fabricated, <OR/>
		2. [ ] has a recent motive to fabricate the statement in court
	3. [ ] the declarant is testifying at trial,
	4. [ ] the declarant is subject to cross-examination,
	5. [ ] the statement was made _before_
	6. [ ] the declarant had a motive to fabricate the statement

##### Explicit Non-hearsay: Prior Statements of Identification
- A statement is not considered hearsay if:
	1. [ ] the statement is a prior identification
		- e.g., a lineup, photo array, etc.
	2. [ ] the proponent is admitting the evidence for substantive purposes,
	3. [ ] the declarant is testifying at trial,
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the declarant is subject to cross-examination

##### Explicit Non-hearsay: Admissions by a Party Opponent
- A statement is not considered hearsay if:
	1. [ ] the statement is made by the opposing party
	2. [ ] in the current litigation

###### Adoptive Admissions
- Silence by the opposing party is considered a statement if:
	1. [ ] the party heard and understood a statement that
	2. [ ] a reasonable person would have denied

###### Vicarious Admissions
- If a statement is made by:
	1. [ ] the opposing party's authorized spokesperson,
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] an agent within
		1. [ ] the scope of an agency relationship with the opposing party, <AND/>
		2. [ ] during an agency relationship with the opposing party
	<OR/>
	3. [ ] a co-conspirator
		1. [ ] during the opposing party's conspiracy, <AND/>
		2. [ ] in furtherance of the opposing party's conspiracy
	- => the statement is considered the opposing party's statement

### Hearsay Exceptions
Hearsay has numerous exceptions. As such, the most contentious checkbox in the [hearsay definition](#definition-of-hearsay) is checkbox 4 — whether the statement falls under an exception. Generally, hearsay exceptions can be divided into two categories:

1. [Exceptions that require unavailable declarants](#exceptions-unavailable-declarant-required),
2. [Exceptions that do not require unavailable declarants](#exceptions-unavailable-declarant-not-required)

#### Exceptions: Unavailable Declarant Required
- All of the exceptions below apply ONLY IF the declarant is unavailable as a witness.

1. former testimony exception,
2. dying declaration exception,
3. statement against interest exception,
4. forfeiture by wrongdoing exception

##### Unavailability Requirement
- A declarant is unavailable only if at least one of these conditions is met:
	1. [ ] the declarant is exempted from testifying because a [privilege applies](#obtaining-opinion-testimony)
	2. [ ] the declarant refuses to testify despite a court order to do so,
	3. [ ] the declarant testifies to not remembering the subject matter,
	4. [ ] the declarant is dead,
	5. [ ] the declarant cannot testify because of physical illness,
	6. [ ] the declarant cannot testify because of mental illness,
	7. [ ] the declarant is:
		1. [ ] absent, <AND/>
		2. [ ] the statement's proponent cannot procure the declarant's attendance through process or other reasonable means

##### Former Testimony
- A statement is not considered hearsay if:
	1. [ ] [the statement's declarant is unavailable](#unavailability-requirement)
	2. [ ] the statement is prior testimony given at either:
		1. [ ] a trial hearing, <OR/>
		2. [ ] a deposition,
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] the opposing party had an opportunity and similar to:
		- [ ] develop the testimony through
			- [ ] cross-examination, <OR/>
			- [ ] direct-examination
- Note that because of condition (3), prior testimony at a grand jury does NOT qualify under the former testimony exception, since the defendant's counsel does not attend grand jury proceedings.

#### Exceptions: Unavailable Declarant Not Required
The following are exceptions that _do not_ require an unavailable declarant. Thus, these exceptions apply whether the declarant is available or unavailable.

##### Present Sense Impression
- If a statement:
	1. [ ] by the declarant
	2. [ ] is a description of an event
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] the statement was made either:
		1. [ ] immediately after the event, <OR/>
		2. [ ] as the event takes place
	- => the statement is admissible as an exception to hearsay

##### Excited Utterance
- If a statement:
	1. [ ] by the declarant
	2. [ ] concerns a startling event
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] the statement was made while
	4. [ ] the declarant was still under stress from the startling event
	- => the statement is admissible as an exception to hearsay

##### Statement of Mind
- If a statement:
	1. [ ] at the time it was made
	2. [ ] evidences either the _declarant's_:
		- [ ] motive
		- [ ] intent
		- [ ] plan
		- [ ] state of mind
		- [ ] emotional condition
		- [ ] sensory condition
		<OR/>
		- [ ] physical condition
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] is offered to prove either the declarant's
		- [ ] state of mind, <OR/>
		- [ ] conduct
	- => the statement is admissible as an exception to hearsay
- Common mistake with this exception:  
	- Counsel wants to prove that the declarant had remembered a particular fact or that they had a particular belief.
	- So, counsel tries to submit a statement about the declarant's memory, past belief, or past state of mind.
	- This is admissible ONLY IF the statement relates to the validity or terms of the declarant's will.

##### Medical Diagnosis or Treatment
- If a statement:
	1. [ ] is about a person's past or present condition
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] the statement was made for the purpose of a medical diagnosis or treatment
	- => the statement is admissible as an exception to hearsay
- Note that these statements are admissible even if they were made to a nurse, family member, or any other non-medical individual.
- They're also admissible even if they're being submitted to enable a physician to testify at trial.

##### Recorded Recollection
- If:
	1. [ ] the witness cannot recall events or information well enough to testify,
	2. [ ] the witness has reviewed the writing while on the stand,
	3. [ ] the record is about a matter the witness once had personal knowledge of,
	<AND/>
	4. [ ] the record was made by or adopted by the witness when the matter was fresh in the witness's mind,
	- => the record may be read into evidence
- Note that only opposing counsel can decide to enter the the record reading as an exhibit.

##### Business Records
- A business record is admissible as a valid exception to the hearsay rule if:
	1. [ ] the record is kept in the course of regularly conducted business, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the record is made by a person
		- [ ] with knowledge of the matter
		- [ ] at or near the time of the matter's occurrence
- Note that business records are not admissible if opposing counsel can show that the source or preparation of the record lacks trustworthiness.
