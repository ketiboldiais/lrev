## Basic Definitions
Because real property law is one of the oldest areas of law, the field suffers from chronic legalese. For proper diagnosis in later sections, this section provides definitions for some common terms.

### Right
- For this outline's purpose, a right is the power or privilege to perform or not perform particular act, for which the right's holder can ask a court to enforce.

### Conveying
- Legalese for "giving."

### Interest
- An interest is the right to own all or part of something.

### Property
- Property is a non-empty set of rights. It includes rights such as:
	- Interest (the right to own)
	- Possession (the right to possess)
	- Entry (the right to access)
	- Alienability (the right to transfer)
	- Enjoyment (the right to use the property as the right holder pleases)
	- Annihilation (the right to destroy)
	- Partition (the right to divide the property into smaller parts)
	- Hypothecation (the right to use the property to secure a debt)
- Property comprises real property, personal property (chattel), and intellectual property (copyrights, trademarks, patents, trade secrets, etc.).  

### Real Property
- Real property is a subset of property. It is the non-empty set of rights to a parcel of land and objects permanently attached to the land (e.g., buildings).

### Title
- Title is a subset of real property, consisting of the following rights:
	1. The right to exclusively possess,
	2. the right to exclusively use,
	3. the right to exclude all others,
	4. the right to transfer,
	5. the right to give an easement,
	6. the right to use the property as secured property,
	7. the right to partition

### Chain of Title
- A _chain of title_ is the chronological order of a given title's successive holders.

### Title Abstract
- A _title abstract_ is a document that records a chain of title.

### Deed
- A _deed_ is a signed legal document transferring property ownership from one party to another.

### Warranty
- In property law, a promise made by a party transferring title (called the _grantor_) to the party receiving the title (called the _grantee_) that:
	1. The grantor, and the grantor's heirs if any, will ensure the title is transferred to the grantee, and
	2. If the grantee is evicted by a third party with paramount title, the grantor will compensate the grantee with other land of equivalent value.
- In contract law:
	1. An express or implied promise by the seller that the property sold is what the seller claims it to be.

### Mortgage
- A mortgage is the transfer of an interest in land as security for an obligation. I.e., if the obligation is not satisfied, the party the obligation is owed to gets to take the land. The _mortgagor_ is the party that transfers the interest in land, and the _mortgagee_ is the recipient.

### Lien
- A lien is the right to keep possession of property belonging to another until a debt owed by the person is discharged.

### Estate
- An _estate_ is the total property (the set of all rights) held by an individual or entity before the property is distributed through a trust or will.

### Real Estate
- Real estate is a subset of estates — it is the total real property (the set of all real property rights) held by an individual or entity before the property is distributed through a trust or will.

### Present Possessory Estate
- A __possessory estate__ (aka _present possessory estate_) is the right to possess a piece of real property _now_. The holder of this right has the present or current right to possess the real property.

### Future Interest
- A future interest is the right to possess property in the future.

### Condition Precedent
- A condition precedent is a condition that must be true for an interest to vest (i.e., the potentialy right-holder can obtain the particular property right only if the condition is met).

### Cotenants
- If property is held by two or more persons together, each property holder is called a cotenant.

### Right of Survivorship
- A right of survivorship is a right that may or may not be provided in jointly-owned property (property owned by two or more people). If the property provides a right of survivorship, then the surviving owner, or owners, automatically absorb the deceased owner's share of the property.
- Example:
	- ${A}$, ${B}$, and ${C}$ own property ${P}$ jointly.
	- So, ${A}$, ${B}$, and ${C}$ each own ${\dfrac{1}{3}}$ of the property.
	- Say ${C}$ dies.
	- Then ${A}$ and ${B}$ each own half of ${C}$'s ${\dfrac{1}{3}}$ share. Thus ${A}$ and ${B}$ each own half of the property:
		$$
		\begin{aligned}
			A(\dfrac{1}{3} + \dfrac{1}{6}) + B(\dfrac{1}{3} + \dfrac{1}{6}) &=
			A(\dfrac{2}{6} + \dfrac{1}{6}) + B(\dfrac{2}{6} + \dfrac{1}{6}) \\[2em]
			&= A(\dfrac{1}{2}) + B(\dfrac{1}{2})
		\end{aligned}
		$$
- Interests from a right of survivorship should always be verified carefully — the right of survivorship is often limited to just a few owners.

### Privity
- When two or more parties transact with one another, their relationship is called a _privity_.

#### Privity of Contract
- When two or more parties enter into an agreement, their relationship is called a _privity of contract_.

#### Privity of Estate
- When two or more parties enter into a lease (e.g. a landlord and a tenant), the relationship between the parties is called a _privity of estate_.

#### Privity of Possession
- When two or more parties successively possess property (e.g., land passed down from grandmother to daughter, daughter to grandaughter, grandaughter to great-grandaughter, etc.) the relationship is called _privity of possession_.

### Chattel
- _Chattel_ is tangible, personal property.

## Present Estates & Future Interests

- In present estates, fee simple is the __default estate__.
- A fee simple is created when the grantor uses any of the following language:
  1. [ ] "${O}$ to ${A}$"
  2. [ ] "${O}$ to ${A}$ and his/her heirs"
  3. [ ] "${O}$ to ${A}$ forever"

### Defeasible Fees

- A __defeasible fee__ is a conveyance in fee simple where the grantor places express conditions on the conveyance.
  - E.g, "${O}$ to ${A}$ but only if..."
  - A defeasibl fee can last forever, but it may be terminated by the occurrence of some event.
- A defeasible fee gives the grantee a __present possessory interest__ in the property, but __reserves a future interest__ in the property favoring the grantor or a third party.
- There are three kinds of defeasible fees, covered below.

#### Fee Simple Determinable (FS-D)

- Grandpa ${G}$ wants to leave his farm to grandson ${A}$, but also wants to ensure the farm stays a farm, rather than some parking lot or apartment complex.
- So, ${G}$ uses language like:
  - "To ${A,}$ while the property is used for farming."
  - "To ${A,}$ during the property's use as a farm"
  - "To ${A,}$ until the property is no longer a farm."
- ${G}$'s scheme is called a __fee simple determinable__ — a conveyance where the grantor keeps the possibility of a reverter open.
- When the conveyance's condition is violated, the property automatically vests back in the grantor.
- The key indicator of an FSD is the use of durational language.
  - "It's yours _while_ ${x}$" where ${x}$ is some condition, circumstance, or fact.

#### Fee Simple Subject to Condition Subsequent (FS-C)

- Grandma ${M}$ wants to leave her ranch to granddaughter ${g}$ because she's getting too old to take care of it. But, ${g}$ doesn't want to deal with the ranch if ${M}$ can take it automatically under her specified conditions.
- So, ${M}$ uses language like:
  - "To ${g}$, _provided that_ the property is used for farming."
  - "To ${g}$, _on the condition that_ the property is used as a farm."
- ${M}$'s scheme is called a __fee simple subject to condition subsequent__.
- Under this scheme, ${M}$ retains the right of entry, but that right does not vest automatically if the condition fails.
- Instead, if ${g}$ violates the conditions, ${M}$ must reclaim the property (e.g., by filing a change of title or making explicit arrangements in their wills or trusts).
- The key indicator of an FS-Csub is the use of conditional language.

#### Fee Simple Subject to Executory Interest (FS-E)

- An FS-E is a conditional conveyance where a third party (not the grantor) is granted an executory interest in the property.
- Grandaunt ${A}$ wants to leave her haunted house attraction to grandniece ${g}$ but wants to make sure it stays a haunted house attraction. ${A}$ knows that grandnephew ${h}$ would keep it a haunted house, but he lives in another state. So, ${A}$ uses the following scheme:
  - "A conveys Scarelane to ${g}$ and her heirs, but if Scarelane is no longer used as a haunted house attraction, then to ${h}$ and his heirs."
- An __executory interest__ is an interest that divests (terminates) an earlier interest.

### Life Estates
- A life estate is another kind of real property right.
- A __life estate__ is a [present possessory estate](#present-possessory-estate) terminated when the _measuring life_ dies.
- Life estates are created when the grantor uses "for ${g}$'s life" language.
- Example 1: "${G}$ to ${A}$ for ${A}$'s life."  
	- ${G}$ creates the life estate.
	- ${A}$ holds the life estate (b.i. possessory estate).
	- ${A}$ is the measuring life.
	- The life estate (b.i. possessory estate) is terminated when ${A}$ dies.
- Life estates are transferrable.
	- Using Example 1, ${A}$ can transfer the life estate to another person ${B.}$
	- Once ${A}$ dies, the life estated transferred to ${B}$ terminates.

#### Future Interest Life Estates
- Life estates can be arranged as a future interest.
	- E.g., "${G}$ to ${A}$ for ${A}$'s life" but only after ${G}$'s death.
- There are two possibilities of future interest life estates:
	1. The estate goes back to the grantor after the life estate terminates. In this scenario, the grantor retains a __reversion__.
	2. The estate does not go back to the grantor after the life estate terminates, but to a third party (e.g., instead of going back to ${G}$, it goes to ${B}$). In this scenario, the third party ${B}$ takes a __remainder__.
		- There are two forms of remainders:
			1. Vested remainders, and
			2. Contingent remainders

##### Vested Remainders
- A vested remainder is a [future interest](#future-interest) that is both:
	1. given to an ascertained (readily-identifiable) third party, AND
	2. not subject to a [condition precedent](#condition-precedent)

##### Contingent Remainders
- A contingent remainder is a future interest that is:
	1. given to a third party that cannot be readily identified, OR
	2. is subject to a condition precedent

## Tenancy-in-common
- A __tenancy-in-common__ is the default estate created by a conveyance of real property to two or more people.
	- e.g., "${P}$ to ${A}$ and ${B}$".
- Each tenant-in-common has the following:
	1. A separate but undivided right to the property (i.e., the property doesn't have to be physically divided)
	2. The right possess and enjoy the entire property, AND
	3. The right to transfer their right in the property freely during their lifetime or at death (b.i., this means there is no right of survivorship)
- If the conveyance of real property to two or more people provides a right of survivorship, then the conveyance is a [joint tenancy](#joint-tenancy).

## Joint-tenancy
- A joint tenancy is a conveyance of real property to two or more people that provides a right of survivorship.  
- When one of the tenants dies, the other joint tenants automatically take the dead tenant's property interest.
- Because of the right of survivorship, joint tenants __cannot__ pass their property interest by will or intestate succession.

### How to Create a Joint-tenancy
- To create a joint tenancy, the grantor must:
	1. [ ] make a clear expression of intent to create a joint tenancy, and
	2. [ ] use survivorship language
		- e.g., "as joint tenants with a right of survivorship"
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] the conveyance satisfies the __four unities__:
		1. [ ] each tenant has an equal right to possess and enjoy the whole property
		2. [ ] each tenant has an equal share of the same type of interest
		3. [ ] each tenant receives their property interests at the same time
		4. [ ] each tenant receives their property interest in the same instrument of title.

### Destroying a Joint-tenancy

- If any of the following occurs in a joint-tenancy:
	1. [ ] at least one tenant does not have an equal right to possess and enjoy the whole property,
	2. [ ] at least one tenant does not have an equal share of the same type of interest,
	3. [ ] at least one tenant receives their property interest at a different time from the others,
	<OR/>
	4. [ ] at least one tenant receives their property interest in a different instrument of title
	- THEN the joint-tenancy is destroyed, and all the cotenants hold the property as a [tenancy-in-common](#tenancy-in-common).
- The two most common ways to destroy a joint-tenancy:
	1. One of the joint-tenants conveys their interest to a third party (the right of survivorship prohibits this).
	2. One of the tenants grants a [mortgage interest](#mortgage) in the joint tenancy to a creditor ([jursidictions are split on this](#granting-a-mortgage-interest-in-a-joint-tenancy)).

#### Granting a Mortgage Interest in a Joint Tenancy
- Does granting a mortgage interest in a joint tenancy destroy the joint tenance?
- Depends on the jurisdiction.
	1. __In lien theory jursdictions__ (majority): The mortgage is a lien and does NOT terminate the joint tenancy.
	2. __Title theory jurisdictions__ (minority): The mortgage will terminate the joint tenancy, and the tenants will then hold the property as tenants in common.

### Rights & Obligations of Cotenants
- Joint-tenants and tenants in common are a type of cotenant.
- Thus, they both share certain rights and obligations.

#### Division of Operating Costs
- Some property — e.g., an amusement park, tourist attraction, etc. — have operating costs like taxes and mortgage payments.
- For cotenants, these costs are divided according to the interest percentage of each cotenant.

#### Rent Payments
- Some jointly-owned property is rented to third parties.
- The rent payments are divided based on the interest percentage of each cotenant.

#### Improvement & Repair Costs
- Even if a property must be repaired or improved, there is no right for reimbursement for repair or improvement costs.
- Thus, if ${A}$, ${B}$, and ${C}$ jointly own some property and ${A}$ pays for all of the repairs/improvements, ${B}$ and ${C}$ have no obligation to pay ${A}$ for the repairs/improvements.
- But, ${A}$ can get credit for their repair/improvement payments in a partition action (essentially relieving ${A}$ of future debt owed to ${B}$ and ${C}$ relating to the property like operating costs).

#### Ouster
- Each cotenant has the right to possess __all__ of the property, regardless of ownership share.
- If one of the cotenants denies another cotenant access to the property, then an __ouster__ occurs.
- The ousted cotenant can:
	1. get an injunction granting access to the property, <OR/>
	2. recover damages for the value of the use while ousted.

#### Partition
- A __partition__ is an equitable remedy available to all cotenants.
- There are two kinds of partitions:
	1. __Partition in kind__: The property is physically divided into distinct portions.  
	2. __Partition by sale__: The property is sold and the proceeds are divided between each cotenant based on their ownership interests. Courts will order a partition by sale only if:
		1. [ ] a partition in kind cannot be done, <OR/>
		2. [ ] a partition in kind would be unfair to _all_ of the cotenants

## Landlords & Tenants
- A landlord is a person or entity that owns real property (most commonly, a house, building, or land) and transfers some of their real property rights to another person or entity through a contract called a __lease__.
- The specifices the transfer's conditions through __covenants__ (promises to do, or not do, certain acts) by both the landlord and the tenant.

### Types of Landlord-tenant Relationships
- Depending on the covenants, a lease creates four possible [present possessory estates](#present-possessory-estate), collectivelly called __leaseholds__:
	1. A tenancy for years (TFY),
	2. A periodic tenancy (PT),
	3. A tenancy at will (TAW), and
	4. A tenancy at sufferance (TAS)
- For each leasehold above, the leasehold can be either:
	1. A __residential lease__, or
	2. A __commercial lease__.

#### Tenancy for Years (TFY)
- A tenancy for years (TFY) is a [possessory estate](#present-possessory-estate) that lasts for a __fixed and ascertainable amount of time__.
- E.g., Landlord ${L}$ leases apartment ${A}$ to student ${S}$ for one year.
- The amount of time the estate lasts is called the leases's __term__ ${t}$, where:

$$
	t = t_f - (t_i + \delta t)
$$

- and ${t_i}$ is the start time of the lease, ${\delta t}$ is the amount of time that has passed since the lease started, and ${t_f}$ is the end time of the lease.

##### Creating a Tenancy for years
- If ${t > 1\text{yr}}$, then the agreement must be in writing because of the statute of frauds.

##### Terminating a Tenancy for Years
- A TFY automatically terminates when the term expires (i.e., ${t = 0.}$).

#### Periodic Tenancy (PT)
- A periodic tenancy is a repetitive and ongoing [possessory estate](#present-possessory-estate) that continues for a set period of time __until__
it is terminated by either party.
- Examples: A month-to-month lease, a year-to-year lease, etc.

##### Creating a Periodic Tenancy
- A periodic tenancy is created only if at least one of the following conditions is met:
	1. [ ] the landlord and the tenant expressly intend to create the PT (e.g., through signed lease agreement), or
	2. [ ] the landlord and the tenant implicitly intend to create the PT (e.g., through ongoing payment of rent)

##### Terminating a Periodic Tenancy
- A periodic tenancy can be terminated only if:
	- [ ] the party seeking termination
	- [ ] gives notice before the start of the last term, AND
- The notice is effective on the last day of the term.

#### Tenancy at Will (TAW)
- A tenancy at will is a [possessory estate](#present-possessory-estate) that continues until it is terminated by either the landlord or the tenant.

##### Creating a Tenancy at Will
- A tenancy at will is created only if:
	1. [ ] the landlord and the tenant expressly intend to create a tenancy at will (e.g., through a signed lease agreement), <OR/>
	2. [ ] the landlord and the tenant implicitly intend to create a tenancy at will (e.g., ongoing payment of rent at will)

##### Terminating a Tenancy at Will
- For tenancies at will, tenants _always_ have the right to terminate, but landlords may or may not have the right.
	- If the lease agreement only gives the landlord the right to terminate at will, the tenant also gets the right to terminate implicitly.
	- If the agreement gives only the tenant the right to terminate at will, the landlord is NOT given the right to terminate at will.

#### Tenancy at Sufferance
- A tenancy at sufferance is a temporary [possessory estate](#present-possessory-estate) where the tenant refuses to vacate the premises after the lease has terminated.

##### Creating a Tenancy at Sufferance
- A tenancy at sufferance is created when:
	1. The lease has terminated, and
	2. the tenant refuses to vacate the premises

##### Terminating a Tenancy at Sufferance
- A tenancy at sufferance is terminated when either of the following conditions are met:
	1. [ ] the landlord evicts the tenant,
	2. [ ] the landlord re-leases the property to tenant, or
	3. [ ] the tenant voluntarily vacates the premises

### Duty to Pay Rent

- The tenant has a contractual duty to pay rent to the landlord in exchange for their possessory interest in the landlord's property.
- If the tenant fails to pay rent, the landlord may evict the tenant or sue the tenant for breach of contract.

#### Exceptions
- If any of the following conditions are true, the tenant has no duty to pay rent:
	1. [ ] the premises are destroyed by a party other than the tenant;
	2. [ ] the landlord completely evicts the tenant;
	3. [ ] the landlord partially evicts the tenant;
	<OR/>
	4. [ ] the landlord materially breaches the lease;

### Implied Warranty of Habitability (IWH)
- For all residential leases, landlords owe an _implied warranty of habitability (IWH)_.
- Under IWH:
	- IF the leased property
		1. [ ] is not reasonably suitable for basic human needs
		2. [ ] because of the landlord's failure to maintain the property
	- THEN the landlord breaches the residential lease and the [tenant may seek legal remedy](#remedies-for-breach-of-lease-via-iwh).
- NOTE: There is no IWH for commercial leases.

#### Remedies for Breach of Lease via IWH
- For all breaches of IWH, the tenant may perform any of the following:
	1. [ ] vacate the premises and terminate the lease,
	2. [ ] withhold or reduce the rent,
	3. [ ] remedy the defect and offset the costs against the rent, <OR/>
	4. [ ] defend against eviction

### Implied Covenant of Quient Enjoyment (ICQE)
- All leases (residential or commercial), landlords owe an _implied covenant of quiet enjoyment (ICQE)_.
- Under ICQE:
	- IF the landlord:
		1. [ ] performs an act that
		2. [ ] makes the premises
			- wholly unsuitable for the premises' intended purpose, <OR/>
			- substantially unsuitable for the premises' intended purpose
		<AND/>
		3. the act causes a [constructive eviction](#constructive-eviction) of the tenant
	- THEN the landlord violates the lease and the [tenant may seek legal remedy](#remedies-for-breach-of-lease-via-icqe).

#### Constructive Eviction
- A constructive eviction occurs if:
	1. [ ] the landlord causes the premises to be unsuitable for their intended purposes,
	2. [ ] the tenant notified the landlord of the problem,
	3. [ ] the landlord did not correct the problem, <AND/>
	4. [ ] the tenant vacated the premises after a reasonable amount of time has passed

#### Remedies for Breach of Lease via ICQE
- For all breaches of ICQE, the tenant may:
	1. [ ] withhold rent, or
	2. [ ] seek damages

### Assignments
- An assignment occurs when:
	- [ ] the tenant transfers their entire remaining term under the lease
	- [ ] to a third party
- EXAMPLE:
	- Landlord ${L}$ leases house ${H}$ to tenant ${T}$ for 1 year.
	- After 6 months, ${T}$ has 6 months remaining on the lease.
	- ${T}$ transfers all their rights under the lease for the remaining ${6}$ months to ${S.}$
	- This is an assignment. ${T}$, the original tenant, is the _assignor_, and ${S}$ is the _assignee_.
- For assignments, the landlord can collect rent from either:
	1. the assignee (because of [privity of estate](#privity-of-estate)), or
	2. the assignor (because of [privity of contract](#privity-of-contract))

### Subleases
- A sublease occurs when:
	- [ ] the tenant transfers less than the entire remaining term under the lease
	- [ ] to a third party
- EXAMPLE:
	- Landlord ${L}$ leases house ${H}$ to tenant ${T}$ for 1 year.
	- After 6 months, ${T}$ has 6 months remaining on the lease.
	- Within those 6 months, ${T}$ will be travelling for 2 months.
	- So, ${T}$ transfers all their rights under the lease for the 2 months to ${S.}$
	- This is a sublease. ${T}$, the original tenant, is the _subleasor_, and ${S}$ is the _subleasee_.
- For subleases, the landlord can only collect rent from the original tenant (because the privity of contract and the privity of estate only exists between the landlord and the original tenant).
- The subleasee only has rent obligations to the subleasor.

### Surrender
- IF:
	1. [ ] a tenant returns possession of the leased premises to the landlord before the expiration of the lease, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the landlord consents
	- THEN:
		- the lease terminates,
		- a landlord-tenant relationship no longer exists,
		- the landlord no longer owes duties and obligations under the lease agremeent, and
		- the tenant no longer owes duties and obligations under the lease agremeent, and

### Abandonment
- IF:
	1. [ ] the tenant unilaterally returns possession of the leased premises,
	2. [ ] before the lease expires, <AND/>
	3. [ ] without the landlords consent
	- THEN:
		- If the landlord has not found a replacement tenant, the abandoning tenant must continue paying rent, <AND/>
		- If the abandoning tenant refuses to pay rent, the landlord is entitled to damages ${D}$:
		$$
			D = (R_o - R_t) - m
		$$
		- where ${R_o}$ is the original rent, ${R_t}$ is the rent received from the replacement tenant, and ${m}$ is the [amount of damages the landlord failed to mitigate](#duty-to-mitigate).

### Duty to Mitigate
- Jursidictions are split on the duty to mitigate.
- __Majority Rule__
	- IF:
		- [ ] before the lease's term ends:
			- [ ] the tenant is evicted <OR/>
			- [ ] the tenant abandons the property
	- THEN:
		- the landlord must make reasonable efforts to re-rent the property to another tenant
- __Minority Rule__
	- The landlord __does not__ have a duty to mitigate damages
	- NOTE: Even within the minority jurisdictions, this rule is generally limited to commercial leases

## Real Covenants
- A real covenant is a promise about how land will be used, binding on successive parties to the real covenant.
- Because real covenants are promises that can span across time, there are two  of parties in a real covenant:
	1. The _promisor_ (the land's owner)
	2. The _promisee_ (the party the original owner conveys the land to)
	3. The _successors_, if any. There are two kinds of successors:
		- The _benefiting successors_ (the promisor's successors in enforcing the promise)
		- The _burdened successors_ (the land's successive holders)
- Real covenants will usually require the holder to either:
	1. do something with the land, or
	2. refrain from doing something with the land.
- Example: ${A}$ sells land ${\ell}$ to ${B,}$ in return for ${B}$'s promise that the land be used as a nature preserve.
- All real covenants have two parts:
	1. A __benefit__ — the ability to enforce the covenant, and
	2. A __burden__ — the obligations or restrictions imposed by the covenant

### Passing Benefits
- For a benefit to pass through to successors, the following conditions must be true:
	1. [ ] __Writing__. The covenant must be included in writing in the origianl conveyance (to satisfy the statute of frauds).
	2. [ ] __Intent__. the original parties must have intended for the covenant to run with the land.
	3. [ ] __Touch and Concern__. The benefit of the covenant must affect both the promisee and promisor as owners of the land (e.g., by increasing the land's value).
	4. [ ] __Relaxed vertical privity__. Each benefiting successor must take an interest carved out of the original party's estate.

### Passing Burdens
- For a burden to pass through to successors, the following conditions must be true:
	1. [ ] __Writing__. The covenant must be included in writing in the original conveyance (to satisfy the statute of frauds).
	2. [ ] __Intent__ . The original parties must have intended for the covenant to run with the land
	3. [ ] __Touch and Concern__. The burden of the covenant must affect both the promisee and promisor as owners (e.g., by increasing the land's value)
	4. [ ] __Strict Vertical Privity__. The successor must take the original party's entire interest.
	5. [ ] __Horizontal Privity__. The conveyance instrument must contain the estate and the covenant (e.g., the deed contains the covenant).
	6. [ ] __Notice__. The new owner must have notice have notice of the covenant, either through:
		1. [ ] actual notice (professional service), OR
		2. [ ] constructive notice (record/publication),

### Remedy: Breach of a Real Covenant
- Claims of a breach of a real covenant are eligible for money damages.

## Equitable Servitudes
- An equitable servitude is a real covenant with the following distinctions:
	1. Claims of a breach of an equitable servitude can only seek injunctive relief.
	2. All of the requirements for real covenants apply, except:
		- None of the privity requirements apply.
		- The notice requirement for burden passing can be satisfied with __inquiry notice__ (e.g., an inspection of the land would reveal the equitable servitude)

## Implied Reciprocal Servitudes

- An implied reciprocal servitude is a restriction land use implied by the land's deed to another.
- Example:
	- ${A}$ divides her land into 4 lots. She sells three lots to ${T}$, ${U}$, and ${V}$ respectively. For each of those sales, the deed provides that the lots may only be used for residential purposes.
	- ${A}$ then sells the last lot to ${W}$, but the deed does not include the same "residential only" provision.
	- If ${W}$ decides to use his lot as a landfill, ${T}$, ${U}$, and ${V}$ can argue that there's an _implied reciprocal servitude_ preventing ${W}$ from using his lot as a landfill.

### Requirements for Implied Reciprocal Servitudes
- For an implied reciprocal servitude to exist, the following conditions must be met:
	1. __Intent__. There must be intent to create aa servitude on all plots (i.e., a common scheme).
	2. __Negativity__. The servitude must result from a promise to refrain from performing certain acts with or on the land.
	3. __Notice__. The party that would be subject to the servitude must have received either:
		1. actual notice,
		2. constructive notice, or
		3. inquiry notice
- Note: Because implied reciprocal servitudes are greated by implication, there is no writing requirement.

## Easements
- An __easement__ is a right held by one person to use another's land.
- There four kinds of easments:
	1. express easements,
	2. implied easements by implication,
	3. implied easements by necesssity,
	4. implied easements by prescription
- The landowner giving the right is the _grantor_, and the party receiving the right is the _grantee_.

### Express Easements
- An express easement is created by either:
	1. [ ] the grantor gives the grantee the right(s) in a writing that satisfies the statute of frauds, OR
	2. [ ] the grantor conveys the land to the grantee, but keeps certain rights for their own use.

### Easement by Implication (EBI)
- An easement by implication is created when all of these conditions are met:
	1. [ ] a _single tract of land is divided by a common owner_,
	2. [ ] one piece of the land is _conveyed_ to another,
	3. [ ] BEFORE the division:
		- the common owner used the single tract of land,
		- as if there was an easement on it,
	4. [ ] AFTER the division:
		- the common owner's use of the conveyed land is continuous and apparent,
		<AND/>
		- such use is reasonably necessary for the owner's use and enjoyment

### Easement by Necessity (EByN)
- An easement by necessity is created when all of these conditions are met:
	1. [ ] a _single tract of land is divided by a common owner_,
	2. [ ] one piece of the land is _conveyed to another_,
	3. [ ] when the land was divided:
		1. [ ] a necessity arose such that
		2. [ ] one of the properties is virtually useless
		3. [ ] without the easement

### Easement by Prescription (EByP)
- An easement by prescription is created when all of these conditions are met:
	1. [ ] a landowner allows a trespasser to
	2. [ ] use their land continuously for the statutory period
	3. [ ] the trespasser's use is:
		1. [ ] hostile (the landowner did not authorize the use), AND
		2. [ ] open and notorious (not hidden),
- NOTE: Unlike adverse possession, the use does not have to be exclusive (e.g., public access to a beach)

### Terminating Easements
- An easement can be terminated by performing any of the following:
	1. RELEASE. An easement is terminated if the grantee expressly release the right in writing and in compliance with the statute of frauds.
	2. MERGER. An easement is terminated if the grantee acquires title to the underlying estate — the easement _merges_ into the title.
	3. ABANDONMENT. An easement is terminated if the grantee demonstrates an intent to never use the easement again through physical action (e.g., moving to another country and never contacting the landowner again; this requires more than non-use or statements).
	4. PRESCRIPTION. An easement is terminated if the grantee fails to protect against trespasses for the statutory period.
	5. SALE. An easement _can_ be terminated if the landowner sells the property (the sale will likely, and should, contain provisions about the easement).
	6. ESTOPPEL. An easement is terminated if the landowner reasonably relies on the grantee's assurance that the easement will no longer be used.
	7. END OF NECESSITY. For EByNs, once the easement is no longer necessary, the easement terminates.

## Profits & Licenses
- A __profit__ is a right to enter another's land to remove a specific natural resource. It is NOT an easement — the rules for easements _do not apply_.
- A __license__ is a _revocable permission_ to user another's land (e.g., a ticket to enter a nature preserve or a music concern). It is NOT an easement — the rules for easements _do not apply_.

## Fixtures
- A fixture is chattel attached to real property in such a way that it is treated as part of the real property.
- Chattel is considered a fixture if:
	- [ ] a reasonable, objective person would conclude that:  
		- [ ] in attaching the chattel to the real property,
		- [ ] the landowner intended for the chattel to become a fixture
- In determining whether the conditions above are met, the court considers:
	1. the importance of the chattel to the real property;
	2. whether the chattel was specially designed for use on the real property; AND
	3. assuming the chattel were removed, the resulting amount of damage to the real property
- NOTE: Structures built on real property (e.g., walls, buildings, sheds, etc.) and materials comprising the structure (e.g. bricks/wood/stone), are fixtures without question.
- Generally, the property owner is the owner of these fixtures as well.

## Requirements for Land Contracts
- A contract for the sale of land is valid only if all these conditions are met:
	1. [ ] if none of the [writing exceptions](#writing-exceptions) apply, the contract is in writing
	2. [ ] the __party to be charged__ (not necessarily the ultimate owner) signs the contract
	3. [ ] the contract contains all of the __essential terms__ (who the parties are, the land's description, terms of price and payment)

### Writing Exceptions
 __Partial Performance Exception__
- A land contract need not be in writing if:
	1. [ ] there is partial performance by either:
		1. [ ] the buyer, or
		2. [ ] the seller
	2. [ ] such partial performance is evidence that the land contract existed
	3. [ ] _at least two_ of the following conditions is true
		- [ ] the buyer now possesses the land
		- [ ] the buyer has paid for all or part of the purchase price
		- [ ] the buyer has made improvements to the land

__Promissory Estoppel__
- A land contract need not be in writing if:
	1. [ ] either:
		- [ ] the buyer, or
		- [ ] the seller
	1. [ ] reasonably relied on the land contract to their detriment
	2. [ ] foreseeably relied on the land contract to their detriment
	<AND/>
	3. [ ] if the court did not enforce the contract, the party seeking enforcement would suffer hardship

### Land Contracts that Merge Covenants
- If a land contract contains covenants, the covenants are _merged_ into the deed.
- If the deed does not contain the covenants, then the covenants cannot be enforced.
- Because of this rule, land contracts comprise three stages:
	1. __The Contract Stage__. The contract is drafted. Liabilities are determined based on the contract's provisions.
	2. __Closing__. The parties sign the contract. The date this occurs is the date the ownership of the property is transferred to the buyer.  
	3. __Deed Stage__. Any liabilities that arise after closing must be based on a _deed warranty_.

### Selling Defective Titles

- For all land contracts, the seller can only convey __marketable title__ to the buyer _at closing_.
- A title is a marketable title only if it is free from an unreasonable risk of litigation. Otherwise, the title is a _defective title_.
- Examples of land with _defective title_:
	1. the land's title was acquired by adverse possession that has not yet been quieted (i.e., supported by a judicial degree),
	2. the land has future interest holders that have not agreed to the transfer
	3. the land has private encumberances (e.g., a mortgage, covenant, option, or easement -- increasingly relevant today: land subject to a homeowner's association),
	4. the land violates a zoning ordinance,
	5. the land has a significant physical defect (e.g., land with hidden toxic waste)
- If the land's title was marketable but later became defective, the defect must be fixed or cured _before closing_.
- If the seller conveys defective title _at closing_, the buyer can rescind the contract without penalty.
- If the seller conveyed marketable title _at closing_ and the land later became defective, the buyer must seek remedy elsewhere.

#### Waivers of Sale of Marketable Title
- The purchaser may choose to waive the requirement that the seller convey marketable title.
- But, even if the purchaser waives this requirement, the seller cannot unilaterally cancel a land sale contract for failure to deliver marketable title.

## Construction/Building Contracts
- Special rules apply to construction contracts and the sale of buildings (or the sale of land with buildings attached).

### Implied Warranty of Fitness (IWF)
- In the real estate context, the _implied warranty of fitness_ is a warranty that arises when a buyer (the customer) requests another to construct a structure, and the seller (the builder) agrees to perform the construction.
- Under IWF, the seller's construction must be safe and fit for human habitation.
- IWF is intended to protect against _latent defects_ (defects that cannot be discovered from a reasonable inspection -- e.g., a building built with improperly mixed concrete).
- __Majority Rule__: both the initial buyer (the party that originally requested the construction) and subsequent buyers can recover damages under IWF.
- __Minority Rule__: only the initial buyer can recover damages under IWF.

### Duty to Disclose Defects
- For most juridictions, sellers have a duty to disclose _material defects_.
- Material defects are defects that substantially impact:
	1. the property's value (e.g., a large pig farm will open nearby soon),
	2. the desirability of the property (e.g., the property was the scene of a murder), or
	3. the health and safety of its occupants (e.g, the building contains asbestos)
- Generally disclaimers ("as is") will NOT satisfy a seller's duty to disclose defects.

### Equitable Conversion & Risk of Loss
- __Majority Rule__: The buyer holds _equitable title_ from (1) the moment of contracting to (2) the deed's delivery.
- During this period:
	1. The buyer is responsible for any damages to the property, AND
	2. the seller, as the legal title holder, has the right to possess the property
- __Minority Rule__: The _Uniform Vendor & Purchaser Risk Act_ applies: The seller bears the risk of loss until closing and delivery of the deed.

## Mortgages

- A mortgage is a contract between a borrower and a lender that ensures the borrower repays their debt.
- The contract consists of two parts:
  1. The borrower's promise to repay the debt, called the __note__,
  2. the __secured property__ - the property the borrower authorizes the lender to sell if they fail to repay the debt, and
  3. the __mortgage__ - the provisions authorizing the lender to sell the secured property if the borrower fails to repay their debt.

### Mortgage Types

- Mortgages fall under two catgories:
  1. __Purchase money mortgages__ are mortgages where the borrower takes out a loan to purchase property (e.g., a home mortgage).
  2. __Future-advance mortgages__ are mortgages where the borrower has access to a line of credit (i.e., the ability to borrow more money after the initial amount) for home equity, construction, business, and commercial loans. (Often called a _second mortgage_ because of the ability to borrow additional amounts).

## Equitable Mortgages
- Equitable mortgages are not really mortgages — they're alternatives to mortgages.
- There are three kinds of equitable mortgages:
	1. __Deed of Trust__. A deed of trust operates like a mortgage, but involves three parties:
		1. the borrrower,
		2. the lender, and
		3. a trustee who holds the property's title until the loan is paid off.
	2. __Installment Land Contact__. The seller finances the purchase and retails title until the buyer makes the final payment on the installment plan (i.e., _redemption_).
	3. __Absolute Deed__. The buyer transfers the property's deed to the lender in exchange for a loan.

### Mortgages: Transferring Secured Property

- Borrowers can transfer title to their property in three ways:
  1. Transferring the property's deed (i.e., selling)
  2. Transferring title through a will
  3. Transferring title through intestate succession
- The party receiving the property is called the _transferee_, and the party giving the property (the borrower) is the _transferor_.
- Even if a borrower transfers the property, they remain personally liable after the transfer UNLESS:
  1. The lender _releases_ the borrower from their repayment obligation, or
  2. The transferee _assumes_ the mortgage (i.e., agrees to take on the repayment obligation) with the lender's approval.

#### Assumption of Mortgage

- When a party receives mortgaged property, there are two possibilities:
  1. The party assumes the mortgage, or
  2. The party receives title _subject to mortgage_ (i.e., does not assume the mortgage)
- If the transferee assumes the mortgage, then:
  1. The transferee is primarily liable in the event of default, and
  2. if the lender cannot collect the debt from the transferee, the original borrow is secondarily liable.
- If the transferee does not assume the mortgage, then:
  1. The transferee is NOT personally liable in the event of default, and
  2. The original borrower remains liable.

#### Acceleration Clauses

- Lenders can demand immediate full repayment when a borrower transfers the secured property through a __due-on-sale clause__.
- Lenders can demand accelerated repayments when a borrower either encumbers the property (e.g., using the property to secure a mortgage elsewhere or to obtain a second mortgage).

### Mortgages: Foreclosure

- A __foreclosure__ is a _forced sale_ of secured property where the sale's proceeds are used to pay off any remaining debt.
- If the sale's proceeds aren't enough to cover the debt, the lender can seek a __deficiency judgment__ against the borrower.

#### Excess Proceeds from Foreclosure
- If there are excess proceeds the money is used to pay off other debts.
- The debts are paid off through the following procedure:

1. Place all of the debts in a list called _all debts_.
2. Sort the debts in both sets chronologically, from earliest to latest.
   - All debts occuring _before_ the foreclosure are called __senior interests__.
   - All debts occuring _after_ the foreclosure called __junior interests__.
3. Starting from the top of _all debts_ (i.e., the oldest debt) to the bottom of _all debts_ (i.e., the most recent debt), perform the following:
   1. If the debt is:
      1. a modification of an earlier debt, AND
      2. the debt is more burdensome on the borrower than the earlier debt,
      - => switch the positions of the modified debt and the earlier debt
   2. If the debt is subordinated to another debt, switch the positions of the two debts.
4. Create two new lists:
   1. _non-purchase-money mortgages_, and
   2. _purchase-money morgages_.
5. Starting from the last debt (the most recent debt) in _all debts_ and ending on the first debt in _all debts_ (the oldest debt), perform the following:
   1. If the debt is a __purchase-money mortgage__, insert it at the top of _purchase-money mortgages list_.
   2. If the debt is an __unrecorded mortage__, insert it below the current bottommost debt in _payment order_.
   3. Else, insert the debt at the top of _payment order_.
6. Merge the _purchase money mortgages_ and _non-purchase-money mortgages_ into a list called _ordered debts_:
   1. `[purchase-money mortgages, non-purchase money mortages]`
7. For each debt in _ordered debts_, pay off the debt with the excess proceeds.

## Adverse Possession

- Adverse possession provides a way for trespassers to acquire land. The idea is: If you don't care enough about your land to ward off trespassers, then the land should be owned by the person who uses it more.
- Adverse possession applies only if all the following conditions are true (_HECO_).
  1. [ ] __Hostile__
      - [ ] the trespasser does not have the true owner's consent to possess or use the property
  2. [ ] __Exclusive__
      - [ ] the trespasser does not share the land with the true owner,
  3. [ ] __Continuous for the statury period__
      1. [ ] the trespasser:
          1. [ ] continously possesses
          2. [ ] land owned by another
          3. [ ] for the specified statutory period
  4. [ ] __Open and notorious__
      - [ ] the trespasser uses the land without hiding from the true owner,

### Hostile

- The trespasser cannot have the true owner's consent to posses or use the property (otherwise they wouldn't be a trespasser).

### Continuous for the Statutory Period

- Seasonal or infrequent use is enough if the use is consistent with the type of property being possessed.
- E.g., the requirement is met if the property is a ski lodge and the trespasser only uses it during the winter months.

#### Tacking

- A trespasser ${t_i}$ can increase the amount of their "possession time" for the statutory period by adding the possession time of a previous trespasser ${t_{i-1},}$ but only if:
  - ${t_{i-1}}$ transferred the land to ${t_i}$ voluntarily.

#### Disabilities

- The trespasser's possession time is reduced by the amount of time — if any — the true owner was disabled _after_ the data the adverse possession started.
- Example:
  - Adverse possession starts in 2006.
  - Statutory period is 5 years.
  - True owner is imprisoned for a crime from 2007 to 2008.
  - Trespasser possesses the land to 2011, when they file a claim.
  - Trespasser does not satisfy the continuity requirement because the 1 year imprisonment is the amount of time the true owner was disabled _after_ the adverse possession started.
  - Trespasser only held it for ${5 - 1 = 4}$ years.

## Deeds
- A __deed__ is a formal document transferring title from one property holder to another.
- The property holder transferring title is called the _grantor_, and the party receiving title is called the _grantee_.

### Deed Requirements
- A deed is valid _only if_ the following conditions are met (_SIDesDA_ ("sigh des da") - statute of frauds, identification, property description, delivery, acceptance):
	1. [ ] __Statute of frauds__.
		1. [ ] the deed is in writing,
		2. [ ] the deed is signed by the grantor,
	2. [ ] __Identification__.
		1. [ ] the deed identifies the grantor.
		2. [ ] the deed indentifies the grantee.
	3. [ ] __Description__.
		- [ ] the deed describes the property sufficiently,
	4. [ ] [__Delivery__](#delivery).
		1. [ ] the grantor demonstrates a _present intent_ to transfer the property
		2. [ ] the transfer is irrevocable
	<AND/>
	6. [ ] [__Acceptance__](#acceptance).
		- [ ] the grantee accepts the deed.

#### Delivery
- Deed does not have to be delivered physically; oral statements are enough.
	- E.g., "Give this deed to ${A.}$"
- Grantor can give the deed through an agent (e.g., having their attorney physically give the deed).
- The delivery requirement is violated if the grantor allows the possibility of revocation.
	- E.g., "You'll have the deed tomorrow, unless I change my mind" is considered revocable, and fails the delivery requirement.
#### Acceptance
- If the property was transferred for value (e.g., a sale), acceptance is presumed.

### Deed Types
- After closing, a land sale contract merges into the deed.
- After this merger, any liability issues concerning the land are resolved with the rules for deeds, _not_ contract.
- Which rules apply depends on the type of deed.
- Generally, there are three types:
  1. Warranty deeds, and
  2. Quitclaim deeds,

#### Warranty Deeds
- A warranty deed is a deed providing six covenants (i.e., promises):
  1. __The Convenant of Seisin__
      - The grantor assures the grantee that the deed describes the land in question, and that they are the rightful owner.
  2. __The Covenant of the Right to Convey__
      - The grantor assures the grantee that they have the right to convey the property.
  3. __The Convenant Against Encumberances__
      - The grantor assures the grantee that there are not undisclosed encumberances on the property that could limit its value (easements, liens, mortgages, etc.).
  4. __The Convenant of Quiet Enjoyment__
      - The grantor, and the grantor's heirs, promise to defend against future challenges to the grantee's title.
  5. __The Convenant of Warranty__
      - The grantor promises to defend against future developments that extend into the grantee's property boundary.
  6. __The Covenant of Further Assurances__
      - the grantor promises to do whatever is reasonably necessary to cure future problems with title.

##### General Warranty Deed
- A __general warranty deed__ is a warranty deed that provides:
  - If the title is defective, the grantor is bound by all six covenants, whether or not the defect was caused by the grantor.

##### Special Warranty Deed
- A __special warranty deed__ is a warrany deed that provides:
  - If the title is defective, the grantor is bound by all six covenants, but _only if_ the grantor caused the defect.

#### Quitclaim Deeds
- A quitclaim deed is a deed where the grantor provides none of the six covenants found in a warranty deed.
- In other words, the grantor makes no promises or assurances that the title is healthy (e.g., that the title is truly theirs, that there aren't any liens, etc.)
