- Almost all crimes require proof of the following four core elements:
	1. [ ] __Actus Reus__. A physical act.
	2. [ ] __Mens Rea__. A mental state.
	3. [ ] __Causation__. A causal connection between the defendant's physical act or failure to act and the harmful result.
	4. [ ] __Concurrence__. The required mental state must have existed at the time of the crime.
- Mnemonic: ACMENCACO - ACt, MENtal state, CAusation, COncurrence.

## Mental State Requirements

- There are three sources of mental statement requirements:
	1. Common law,
	2. Statutory law, and
	3. The MPC (Model Penal Code)

### Common Law

- Under common law, there are four different requisite mental states, depending on the crime:

- Defenses to specific intent crimes (e.g., voluntary intoxication) do not apply to malice crimes.

- General intent is a catch-all category of intent.
- To demonstrate general intent, the State must prove that:
  - [ ] the defendant intended to commit an act
  - [ ] prohibited by law
- Note: Whether the defendant intended the act's result is irrelevant.
- General intent crimes include:
  - [ ] [battery](#battery),
  - [ ] [rape](#common-law-rape),
  - [ ] [voluntary manslaughter](#voluntary-manslaughter-heat-of-passion),
  - [ ] [involuntary manslaughter](#involuntary-manslaughter),
  - [ ] kidnapping, and
  - [ ] [false imprisonment](#false-imprisonment)

- Strict liability crimes are crimes that only require the state to show:
  - [ ] the defendant voluntarily committed the crime
- Defenses that negate a state of mind do not apply.
- With strict liability crimes, the defendant's actual intent is irrelevant.
- All that matters is that they voluntarily committed the acts comprising the crime.
- Strict liability crimes include:
  - [ ] [statutory rape](#statutory-rape),
  - [ ] selling liquor to minors, and
  - [ ] polygamy

- Under the MPC, there is no distinction between general and specific intent.
- Instead, there are 4 categories of intent:
	1. __purposely__
	2. __knowingly or willfully__
	3. __recklessly__
	4. __negligently__

#### Purposely

- A defendant acts "purposely" when their conscious objective is to engage in the conduct or to cause a certain result.

#### Knowingly or Willfully

- A defendant acts "knowingly or willfully" when the defendant knows their conduct is required by a crime, or that the circumstances surrouding their conduct are required by a crime.

#### Recklessly

- A defendant acts "recklessy" when they act with a __conscious disregard of substantial and justifiable risk__ that a material element of a crime exists or will result from their conduct.
- The risk must constitute a gross deviation from the standard of conduct of a law abiding person.

#### Negligently

- A defendant acts "negligently" when they __fail to perceive a substantial and unjustifiable risk__ that a material element of a crime exists or will result from their conduct. The risk must constitute a gross deviation from the standard of a reasonable person in the same situation.

## Criminal Homicide

- A __criminal homicide__ occurs when one person causes the death of another human being.
- The penalties for homicide deepend on (1) the legal classification of the crime, and (2) the circumstances surrounding the death.
- There are four legal classes of homicides:
	1. common law criminal homicides, and
	2. statutory homicides
- Common law criminal homicides are homicides governed under common law.
- Statutory homicides are homicides governed by statute.
- For most, if not all, jurisdictions, homicides are governed by statute. Nevertheless, principles and rules from common law are still applied in statutory interpretation and application.

### Common Law Murder

- A __murder__ is the unlawful killing of a human being with __malice aforethought__.
- Malice aforethought is the _mens rea_ requirement for common law murder.
- To convict a defendant accused of common law murder, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] there are no facts:
		 - [ ] reducing the killing to voluntary manslaughter, <OR/>
		 - [ ] excusing the killing,
			 <AND />
	2. [ ] one of the following states of mind:
		 - [ ] the defendant intended to kill,
		 - [ ] the defendant intended to inflict great bodily injury,
		 - [ ] the defendant was recklessly indifferent to an unjusitifably high risk to human life (a __depraved heart murder__), <OR/>
		 - [ ] the defendant intended to commit a felony (the __felony murder rule__)
- Note that most states [modify common law murders via statute](#statutory-homicide).

#### Voluntary Manslaughter (Heat of Passion)

- A __voluntary manslaughter__ is a killing that would be murder, but for the existence of __adequate provocation__.
- In other words, if it weren't for the victim provoking the defendant, the killing would be a common law murder.
- To convict a defendant under voluntary manslaughter, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] the provocation is one that would cause __sudden and intense passion__ in an ordinary person's mind,
	2. [ ] the provocation is one that would cause the ordinary person to lose self control, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the time between the provocation and the killing was not enough for an ordinary person to __cool off__.

##### Sudden and Intense Passions

- Examples of sudden and intense passions include:
	1. being confronted with a deadly force (e.g., the victim aimed a gun at the defendant)
	2. adultery - finding a spouse in bed with another[^adultery_note],
		 - Note that some courts have held that the defendant must have actually found the victim in bed with their spouse.[^adultery_note2]
		 - E.g., the fact the defendant saw the victim climbing out their spouse's window is insufficient.
	3. being the victim of a batter, mutual combat, or aggravated assault
	4. illegal arrest

[^adultery_note]: See _R. v. Mawgridge_, (1706) Kelyng. J.119.
[^adultery_note2]: See _State v. John_, 30 N.C. (8 lred.) 330 (1848).

##### Cooling Off

- The _cool off_ requirement is based on the assumption that if the more time there is between the provocation and the killing, the more likely it is that the defendant has calmed down.
- The amount of time sufficient for the defendant to cool off is a __question of fact__. Thus, it's up to the jury to decide whether the time was sufficient.

##### Rekindling

- Some courts have held that even if there was sufficient time after cooling off, the defendant's "heat" can be rekindled because of another event.[^rekindle_note]

[^rekindle_note]: _See_ Aya Gruber, Victim Wrongs: The Case for a General Criminal Defense Based on Wrongful Victim Behavior in an Era of Victims’ Rights, 76 Temp. L. Rev. 645, n. 114 (2003) (California and Pennsylvania recognize a "rekindling" theory).

#### Involuntary Manslaughter

- Involuntary manslaughter is an __unintentional killing__ committed:
	1. [ ] with [criminal negligence](#criminal-negligence)
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] during the commission of a misdemeanor,
	<OR/>
	3. [ ] during the commission of a felony that:
  - is not classified as 1st-degree murder by statute, <AND/>
  - is not classfied as 2nd-degree murder by statute

### Statutory Homicide
- As mentioned earlier, most states address homicide via statute. Some of these statutes classify homicides in degrees, the two most common of which are:
	1. First-degree murder,
	2. Second-degree murder,
	3. Third-degree murder

#### First-degree Murder
- First-degree murder is a [common-law murder](#common-law-murder) with any one of the following elements:
	1. [ ] premeditation (a [premeditated murder](#premeditated-murder)),  
	2. [ ] the killing was done during the commission of an enumerated felony (a [felony murder](#felony-murder)),
	<OR/>
	3. [ ] the killing was performed in a heinous manner ([a heinous murder](#heinous-murder))

##### Premeditated Murder
- For the State to convict a defendant under premeditated murder, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the murder was deliberate and premeditated (i.e., planned),
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant made the decision to kill in a cool and dispassionate manner, and actually reflected on the idea of killing

##### Felony Murder
- To convict a defendant under felony murder, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant killed the victim:
			- [ ] during the course of an [enumerated felony](#enumerated-felonies)
			<OR/>
			- [ ] during the attempt to commit an [enumerated felony](#enumerated-felonies);
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] [the defendant committed or attempted to commit the underlying felony](#actual-committment-or-attempt-of-felony), <AND/>
	3. [ ] [the felony is distinct from the killing itself](#felony-must-be-distinct), <AND/>
	4. [ ] [the victim's death was a foreseeable result of the felony](#foreseeable-result), <AND/>
	5. [ ] [the death was caused before the defendant's immediate flight from the felony's conclusion](#immediate-flight)
- Example: ${A,}$ in the process of raping ${B,}$ kills ${B.}$

###### Enumerated Felonies
- Which felonies are enumerated depends on the jurisdiction. The most common felonies are:
  - burglary,
  - robbery,
  - arson,
  - kidnapping,
  - sex crimes
- A helpful acronym: (BRAKES)
- Generally, enumerated felonies are dangerous felonies — i.e., pose a threat of great bodily harm. Crimes that are not dangerous — e.g., fraud — are not enumerated felonies.[^enumerated_felony_note]

[^enumerated_felony_note]: _See_ _People v. Washington_, 62 Cal. 2d. 777 (1965).

###### Actual Committment or Attempt of Felony
- To be convicted under felony murder, the defendant must have actually committed or attempted to commit the felony.
- This means that the state must not only prove the other elements of the other murder, it must also prove the elements of the underlying felony.
- Example:
  - ${A}$ and ${B,}$ in the process of robbing ${C,}$ murder ${C.}$
  - To convict ${A}$ under felony murder, the state must prove that ${A}$ committed or attempted to commit the robbery.

###### Felony Must Be Distinct
- To be convicted under felony murder, the State must prove that the alleged felony is distinct from the killing itself.
- Example:
  - ${A}$, in the process of punching ${B,}$ kills ${B.}$
  - The underlying felony — a battery — is not distinct from the killing.

###### Foreseeable Result
- There are usually no issues with this prong. Courts are usually liberal about defining what is foreseeable.[^foreseeable_note]
- Example:
  - ${A}$ and ${B}$ are in the process of kidnapping ${C.}$
  - ${D,}$ a security guard, chases after ${A}$ and ${B.}$
  - In the process, ${D}$ is run over by a car.
  - Courts will usually find that it is foreseeable for someone to chase after ${A}$ and ${B,}$ and that it's foreseeable for something to happen to someone during such a chase.

[^foreseeable_note]: _See_ _State v. Noren_, 371 N.W.2d 381 (Wis. 1985).

###### Immediate Flight
- To be convicted under felony murder, the State must prove that the cause of the death occurred before the defendant reached a place of temporary safety.[^immediate_flight_note].
- Example 1:
  - ${A}$, ${B}$, and ${C}$ plan to rob Bank.
  - The three successfully rob the bank.
  - ${A,}$ the getaway driver starts driving to their hideout, a warehouse.
  - ${A}$ loses control of the car and crashes into a van with two kids.
  - The kids are killed.
  - Here, ${A,}$ ${B,}$ and ${C}$ are on the hook for felony murder because the deaths occurred before they reached their hideout.
- Example 2:
  - Same facts as Example 1, only this time, ${A,}$  ${B,}$ and ${C}$ reach the hideout safely.
  - They stay at the hideout overnight.
  - The next day, ${A}$ drives them to their next hideout.
  - Along the way, ${A}$ falls asleep at the wheel and crashes into a van with two kids.
  - The kids are killed.
  - ${A,}$ ${B,}$ and ${C}$ are not on the hook for felony murder because the kids' deaths did not occur during their immediate flight from the robbery.

[^immediate_flight_note]: See _People v. Lopez_, 6 Cal. 3d 45 (1971).

##### Heinous Murder
- A heinous murder is a murder __performed in a certain manner__.
- Generally, these are murders that shock the conscience.
- Examples of such manners include:
  - killing by torture,
  - killing by mutilation,
  - killing designed to inflict a high degree of pain

#### Second-degree Murder
- Generally, a second-degree murder is a murder that:
	1. [ ] is an unplanned, intentional killing.
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] caused by a reckless disregard for human life
- The analogs in common law are:
  - [Voluntary Manslaughter](#voluntary-manslaughter-heat-of-passion)
    - Example: ${A}$ beats ${B}$ after finding ${B}$ in bed with her husband.
  - [Common law murder, prong 4](#common-law-murder)
    - Example: ${A}$ drives drunk and runs over ${B.}$

#### Third-degree Murder
- Generally, a third-degree murder is an unplanned, unintentional killing that is _not_ part of another felony.
- The analog in common law is [involuntary manslaughter through an unlawful act](#involuntary-manslaughter)
  - Example: ${A}$ punches ${B}$ and kills ${B.}$
- For some jurisdictions, third-degree murder also covers "accidental killings".
  - Example: ${A}$ trips and pushes ${B}$ off a cliff, killing ${B.}$

### Causation in Criminal Homicide
- To convict a defendant under criminal homicide, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant's conduct was the [cause-in-fact](#cause-in-fact) of the victim's death, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the defendant's conduct was the [proximate cause](#proximate-cause) of the victim's death

#### Cause-in-fact
- To prove that the defendant's conduct was the cause-in-fact of the victim's death, the state must prove that:
  - [ ] but for the defendant's conduct, the victim's death would not have occured.
- I.e., if it weren't for the defendant's conduct, the victim wouldn't have died.

#### Proximate Cause
- To prove that defendant's conduct was the proximate cause of the victim's death, the state must prove that:
  - [ ] the victim's death was a natural and probable consequence of the defendant's conduct.
- This is an important element to keep in mind.
- Events can break the chain of causation, negating this element.
- Events that are not generally foreseeable will shield the defendant.
- Examples:
  - ${A}$ punches ${B,}$ and out of nowhere, ${C}$ is struck by lighting.
  - ${A}$ punches ${B,}$ and ${C}$ runs over ${B}$ after falling asleep at the wheel.

##### Subsequent Medical Treatment
- A third party's negligent medical care is considered a foreseeable risk. Thus, subsequent negligent medical care that causes the victim's death is not a defense.
  - Example: ${A}$ beats ${B}$ with a bat. ${B}$ goes to the hospital, and their doctor, ${C,}$ negligently administers the wrong dose of anesthesia, killing ${B.}$ The fact that ${B}$ died because of ${C}$'s negligence does not take a murder charge against ${A}$ off the table.
- The victim's refusal of medical treatment for religious reasons is also considered a foreseeable risk. Thus, the fact that the victim's refusal caused their death is not a defense either.

## Battery
- For the State to convict a defendant under criminal battery, the state must demonstrate:
	1. [ ] the defendant [applied force](#application-of-force),
	2. [ ] to the [person of another](#person-of-another),
	3. [ ] resulting in [bodily harm or offensive contact](#resulting-in-bodily-harm-of-offensive-contact),
	4. [ ] without [legal excuse](#legal-excuse-justification-defenses), <AND/>
	5. [ ] with [general intent](#general-intent)

### Application of Force
- Force can be applied by a third party acting under the defendant's direction, or by an object controlled by the defendant.

### Person of Another
- To be convicted of battery, the defendant must have applied force to one of the following:
	1. [ ] an object near the victim,
	2. [ ] an object carried by the victim,
	<OR/>
	3. [ ] an object attached to the victim

### Resulting in Bodily Harm of Offensive Contact
- To be convicted of battery, the defendant's application of force must result in one of the following:
	1. [ ] a bodily harm (e.g., a bruise), or
	2. [ ] an offensive contact (e.g., an unwanted kiss)

## Aggravated Battery
- For most jurisdictions, aggravated battery is a more serious (and thus treated as a felony) form of battery.
- To convict a defendant of aggravated battery, the State must show that:
	1. [ ] the defendant [applied force](#application-of-force),
	2. [ ] to the [person of another](#person-of-another),
	3. [ ] resulting in [bodily harm or offensive contact](#resulting-in-bodily-harm-of-offensive-contact),
	4. [ ] without [legal excuse](#legal-excuse-justification-defenses),
	5. [ ] with [general intent](#general-intent), <AND/>
	6. [ ] any one of the following conditions:
		- [ ] the act was committed with a deadly weapon,
		- [ ] the act resulted in __great bodily harm or offensive contact__, <OR/>
		- [ ] the act was committed against a child, woman, or police officer.

## Assault
- To convict the defendant of criminal assault, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] the defendant attempted to commit a [battery](#battery),
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant intentionally placed another in apprehension of imminent bodily harm
- Note that if the defendant has made physical contact with the victim, the crime can only be battery — not assault.

## Aggravated Assault
- Most jurisdictions distinguish more serious assaults as _aggravated assault_.
- To confict a defendant of aggravated assault, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] the defendant attempted to commit a [battery](#battery) __with a deadly weapon__,
	<OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant intentionally placed another in apprehension of imminent bodily harm __with a deadly weapon__

## False Imprisonment
- To convict a defendant under false imprisonment, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] the defendant confined the victim,
	2. [ ] without the victim's valid consent, <AND/>
	3. [ ] there is no [legal excuse](#legal-excuse-justification-defenses) for the confinement
	4. [ ] [general intent](#general-intent)
- The model penal code requires one more element:
  - [ ] the confinement must have interfered substantially with the victim's liberty

### Confinement
- There are many ways to _confine_ a person. It doesn't necessarily mean forcing someone to stay in an enclosed space:
  - forcing a person to go where they don't want to go
  - preventing someone from going where they want to go
  - blocking escape/alternate routes

### Substantial Interference with Liberty
- Under the model penal code, the confinement must have interfered with the victim's substantial liberty.
- Thus, if the defendant had alternative routes — escape routes — a false imprisonment conviction is unlikely.

## Common Law Rape
- To convict a defendant under common law rape, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] [the defendant and the victim are not married](#spousal-immunity),
	2. [ ] the victim was a female,
	3. [ ] the defendant had sexual intercourse with the victim,
	4. [ ] [without the victim's effective consent](#lack-of-consent)
	4. [ ] [general intent](#general-intent)
- Most jurisdictions today cover rape offenses through [sexual assault](#sexual-assault).

### Spousal Immunity
- Under common law and the Model Penal Code, a husband cannot rape his wife.
- For the vast majority of jurisdictions, spousal immunity no longer holds per [sexual assault statutes](#sexual-assault).

### Lack of Consent
- To convict a defendant for rape, the State must prove that the victim did not give effective consent. This is done by showing one of the following:
	1. [ ] the defendant forced the victim to have sex with him;
	2. [ ] the victim had sex with the defendant because the defendant threatened great and immediate bodily harm;
	3. [ ] the victim could not consent because of:
			- [ ] unconsciousness;
			- [ ] intoxication; <OR/>
			- [ ] a mental condition
	<OR/>
	4. [ ] the defendant fraudulently caused the victim to believe the act was not sexual intercourse

## Sexual Assault
- States vary in their requirements for sexual assault. In general:
	1. [ ] the defendant had sexual contact with the victim,
	2. [ ] without the victim's effective consent.
- Sexual contact is a broad term, and includes virtually any physical contact — it's not limited to penetration.
- Most sexual assault statutes also get rid of spousal immunity under common law, and remove the requirement that the victim be female.

## Statutory Rape
- To convict a defendant for statutory rape, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant had sexual intercourse,
	2. [ ] with a person under age
- Statutory rape is a strict liability crime.
- Thus, a defendant's reasonable [mistake of fact](#mistake-of-fact) about the victim's age (e.g., "She told me she was 18") is NOT a defense.

## Larceny
- To convict a defendant for larceny, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] [the defendant obtained possession](#taking),
	2. [ ] [and carried away](#asportation),
	3. [ ] [the personal property of another](#personal-property),
	4. [ ] by trespass,
	5. [ ] with intent to permanently deprive.

## Embezzlement
- To convict a defendant for embezzlement, the State must prove:
	1. [ ] [a fraudulent conversion](#fraudulent-conversion),
	2. [ ] of the personal property of another,
	3. [ ] [by a person in lawful possession of that property](#possession-of-property)
	4. [ ] [the defendant intended the fraudulent conversion](#fraudulent-intent)

### Fraudulent Conversion
- A fraudulent conversion occurs when the defendant deals with the property in a manner inconsistent with the arrangement by which the defendant has possession.
- I.e., the victim gave the defendant the property under the assumption that the defendant would use it for ${x,}$ and the defendant uses it for something other than ${x.}$

### Fraudulent Intent
- Embezzlement is a [specific intent](#specific-intent) crime. The defendant must intend to defraud.

### Possession of Property
- To convict a defendant for embezzlement, the defendant must be in [__lawful possession__](#custody-vs-possession) of the property when the conversion occurs.
- Thus, [custody](#custody-vs-possession) is insufficient.

## False Pretenses
- To convict a defendant for false pretenses, the state must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant obtained title,
	2. [ ] to the personal property of another,
	3. [ ] [by an intentional false representation](#intentional-false-representation),
	4. [ ] of a [material past or present fact](#material-or-present-fact),
	5. [ ] with [intent to defraud](#intent-to-defraud)

### Intentional False Representation
- To convict a defendant for false pretenses, the victim must have:
	1. [ ] relied on the false representation, and
	2. [ ] that reliance caused the victim to give the defendant title to the property

### Material or Present Fact
- To convict a defendant for false pretenses, the defendant must have falsely represented:
	1. [ ] a material past fact, <OR/>
	2. [ ] a material present fact
- This means that opinions do not count.

### Intent to Defraud
- False pretenses is a [specific intent](#specific-intent) crime.
- To convict a defendant for false pretenses, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant knew the statement they made was false, <OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant intended the victim to rely on the misrepresentation
- For most states, the first condition is satisfied if the defendant:
	1. [ ] was put on notice that the statement was highly likley false, and
	2. [ ] the defendant deliberately avoided learning the truth

## Robbery
- To convict a defendant for robbery, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant [took](#taking) or [carried away](#asportation),
	2. [ ] the [personal property of another](#personal-property)
	3. [ ] from the other's person or presence,
	4. [ ] by force or threat of force,
	5. [ ] with the intent to [permanently deprive](#intent-to-permanently-deprive)

## Receipt of Stolen Property
- To convict a defendant for receipt of stolen property, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant received [possession](#possession-of-property) and control,
	2. [ ] of stolen [personal property](#personal-property),
	3. [ ] known to have been obtained in an unlawful manner,
	4. [ ] by another person,
	5. [ ] with the intent to [permanently deprive the owner of the property](#intent-to-permanently-deprive)

## Burglary
- To convict a defendant for burglary, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] at nighttime,
	2. [ ] the defendant [broke into](#breaking) [another's dwelling](#dwelling-of-another),
	3. [ ] the defendant [entered](#entry) [another's dwelling](#dwelling-of-another),
	4. [ ] the defendant intended to a commit a felony in the structure,
	5. [ ] at the time of entry
- Notice that burglary != (theft || robbery).
- The crime of burglary is breaking and entering into a nother person's home with the intent to commit a felony. That felony could be arson, murder, rape, etc.

### Breaking
- For a defendant to be convicted of burglary, the State must prove that there was a breaking. To do so, it must show that:
	1. [ ] the defendant created or expanded an opening by
	2. [ ] at least minimual force, fraud, or intimidation

### Entry
- For a defendant to be convicted of burglary, the State must prove that there was an entry. To do so, it must show that:
	1. [ ] the defendant placed:
			- [ ] a portion of their body, <OR/>
			- [ ] an instrument used to commit the crime
	3. [ ] into the structure

### Dwelling of Another
- A dwelling is a structure used regularly for sleeping purposes, even if it's used for other purposes such as conducting a business.
- Ownership of the dwelling is irrelevant — the only requirement is that the structure is occupied by someone other than the defendant.

## Common Elements of Property Crimes
- Below is an overview of some of the common elements of property crimes.

### Asportation
- The carried away requirement is satisfied by the slightest movement (e.g., just a few inches).
- Asportation is an element of:
	1. [larceny](#larceny)
	2. [robbery](#robbery)

### Taking
- For a defendant to be convicted of larceny, the defendant must have _taken_ the property from another.
- If the defendant already had possession of the property, there is no taking. But it may be [embezzlement](#embezzlement).

### Personal Property
- To be convicted of larceny, the defendant must have taken _personal property_ — goods and chattels.
- Thus, the personal property element is not met if the defendant took _real property_ — land, fixtures (items permanently affixed to the land).[^state_v_jackson]
  - But, once fixtures, crops, or minerals are severed from the realty, they become personal property can be subject to larceny.[^state_v_jackson_id]

[^state_v_jackson]: _See_ _State v. Jackson_, 11 S.E.2d 149 (N.C. 1940).
[^state_v_jackson_id]: _Id._

#### Abandoned, Lost, or Mislaid Property
- Larceny can be committed with lost property.
- Larceny can also be committed with mislaid property (property mistakenly delivered).
- Larceny __cannot__ be committed with abandoned property.

### Intent to Permanently Deprive
- To convict the defendant under larceny, the state must prove that:
  - [ ] at the time of the taking,
  - [ ] the defendant intended to permanently deprive the victim of their personal property.
- Crimes with this element include:
	1. [ ] larceny
	2. [ ] robbery

#### Intent to Restore
- If the defendant intends to restore the __exact property__ taken (i.e., return the property to the victim), there is no embezzlement.
- The defense of intent to restore does not apply if the property restored is merely similar or substantially identical.
  - This implies that intent to restore rarely applies to embezzlement of money, since the original money is likely elsewhere.

### Custody vs. Possession
- Possession involes a greater scope of authority to deal with the property than custody.
- Generally:
  - A person has __possession__ of property if they have discretionary authority over the property.
  - A person has __custody__ of property if they only have limited authority over the property.
- Example: Company employees generally only have custody of an employer's property. The company, however, has possession over such property.

## Inchoate Offenses
Inchoate offenses are crimes where liability attaches even if the crime has not been completed. Generally, these crimes are steps towards other crimes.

### Attempt
- To convict a defendant for an attempt, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant had [specific intent](#specific-intent) to commit a crime, <AND/>
	2. [ ] there was an overt act beyond mere preparation that falls short of completing the crime

#### Merger of Attempt
- Attempt always merges with an underlying crime.
- In other words, a person cannot be convicted for both attempt _and_ the underlying crime.

#### Abandoned Attempt
- Abandonment or withdrawal occurs when the defendant stops the attempt.

##### Common Law View of Abandoned Attempt
- Under common law, abandonment or withdrawal is __not__ a defense to attempt.
- If the defendant had the intent and committed an overt act beyond mere preparation, the defendant is guilty of attempt even if they changed their mind and abandoned the plan before the crime was completed.

##### MPC View of Abandoned Attempt
- Under the MPC (a minority of jurisdictions), if the defendant:
	1. [ ] voluntarily, and
	2. [ ] completely abandons
	3. [ ] the attempt
		- => the defendant has a valid defense against an attempt charge.
- BUT: Abandonment is not voluntary if it's motivated by a desire to avoid detection.

### Solicitation
- To convict a defendant of solicitation, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] the defendant requested another to commit a crime,
	2. [ ] with the intent that the person solicited commit the crime

#### Merger of Solicitation  
- For more jurisdictions, solicitation merges with the substantive offense.
- Thus, a defendant cannot be convicted for both solicitation _and_ the underlying crime.

#### Renunciation
- Renunciation occurs when the solicitor renounces or widthraws the solicitation.

##### Majority View of Renunciation
- For most jurisdictions, renunciation is __not__ a defense against solicitation.

##### MPC View of Renunciation
- Under the MPC, renunciation is a valid defense if:
  - [ ] the defendant prevents the commission of the crime

### Conspiracy
- To convict a defendant of conspiracy, the State must prove that:
	1. [ ] there was an agreement between two or more persons,
	2. [ ] to accompolish a criminal objective,
	3. [ ] with the intent to enter into the agreement, <AND/>
	4. [ ] with the intent to commit the criminal objective
	5. Most jurisdictions also require:
		1. [ ] an [overt act](#overt-act)

#### Overt Act
- For the majority of jurisdictions, to convict a defendant of conspiracy, the State must demonstrate that:
  - [ ] there was an overt act in furtherance of the crime
- The overt act does not have to be criminal in nature.
  - E.g., a conspirator buying gasoline to commit arson is sufficient as an overt act, even though buying gasoline is not criminal in nature.

#### Termination
- A conspiracy terminates once the criminal objective is completed.
- All acts or statements made __after__ the termination are inadmissible against a conspirator.
- Example:
  - ${A}$ and ${B}$ conspire to rob a convenience store.
  - ${A}$ and ${B}$ successfully rob the store, but are caught the next day.
  - The statements ${A}$ and ${B}$ make to the police are inadmissible against ${A}$ and ${B}$ for the purposes of the conspiracy charge.

#### Pinkerton Liability
- A conspirator may be held liable for crimes committed by their co-conspirators if the crimes were:
	1. [ ] committed in furtherance of the criminal objective, <AND/>
	2. [ ] foreseeable
- EXAMPLE:
  - ${A}$ and ${B}$ conspire to beat up ${C.}$
  - In the process, ${B}$ kills ${C.}$
  - Even if ${A}$ did not kill ${C,}$ ${A}$ can be held liable for ${B}$'s crime as a co-conspirator.

#### Abandoned Conspiracy
- Abandonment occurs when a conspirator withdraws or refuses to further participate in the conspiracy.
- Abandonment is a defense ONLY IF the conspirator:
	1. [ ] performs an _affirmative act_ that notifies all the other conspirators of their withdrawal,
	2. [ ] gives notice in time for the members to abandon their plans, <AND/>
	3. [ ] attempts to neutralize any assistance they provided

#### Merger of Conspiracy
- Conspiracy __does not__ merge with the completed crime.
- Thus, the defendant may be convicted of the crimes committed in furtherance of the conspiracy __and__ the conspiracy itself.

## Criminal Responsibility
Even if the prosecution can successfuly prove all the elements an offense, it must still consider _criminal responsibility_. In general, this involves answering two questions:

1. Who is responsible for the crime?
2. Can the defendant be held responsible?

### Who is Responsible for the Crime?
- Under modern statutes, there are three potential parties to a crime:
	1. The [principal](#the-principal)
	2. An [accomplice](#accomplice)
	3. An [accessory](#accessory)

#### The Principal
- The principal is the person who commits the illegal act or who causes an innocent agent to do so.

#### Accomplice
- An accomplice is a person who aids or encourages the principal to commit an illegal conduct.
- If an accomplice:
	1. [ ] aids, abets, or facilitates the commission of a crime committed by the principal,
	2. [ ] with the intent that the crime be committed,
  - => the accomplaice is liable for all the crimes that:
		1. the principal committed <AND/>
		2. all other crimes that were a __probable__ or __foreseeable__ result.

##### Defense: Repudiation
- Repudiation is the defense of having withdrawn from being an accomplice.
- Whether repudiation is a valid defense depends on the degree of the accomplice's involvement.
- If the accomplice's involvement was mere encouragement, then repudiation is a valid defense only if:
	1. [ ] the accomplice withdraws their involvement,
	2. [ ] __before__ the crime becomes unstoppable
- If the accomplice's involvement went beyond mere encouragement (e.g., actively assisting the principal), then repudiation is a valid defense only if:
	1. [ ] the accomplice withdraws their involvement,
	2. [ ] __before__ the crime becomes unstoppable, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the accompliced attempted to neutralize their assistance

#### Accessory
- An accessory is a person who aids another to escape knowing that they have committed a felony.
- An accessory is only liable for the less serious offense of being an accessory.

### Can the Defendant Be Held Responsible?
- Even if the State successfully proves, or can successfully prove, all the elements of an offense and obtain a conviction, __criminal capacity rules__ can result in an acquittal.

#### Fitness to Stand Trial
- To convict a defenant, the defendant must be __competent to stand trial__.
- To be competent, the State must satisfy the _Dusky Test_:
	1. [ ] the defendant has sufficient present ability to consult with their lawyer with a reasonable degree of rational understanding, <AND/>
	2. [ ] the defendant has a rational and factual understanding of the proceedings against them.

#### Insanity
- A defendant may be entitled to an acquittal if:
	1. [ ] at the time of the crime
	2. [ ] the defendant was __legally insane__.
- There are four tests for determining whether a defendant is legally insane:
	1. [The M'Naghten Rule](#mnaghten-rule)
	2. [Irresistible Impulse Test](#irresistible-impulse-test)
	3. [Model Penal Code Test](#model-penal-code-test)
	4. [Durham Test](#durham-test)

##### M'Naghten Rule
- Under the _M'Naghten Rule_ a defendant is entitled to an aquital because of legal insanity if:
	1. [ ] the defendant did not know the nature and quality of the unlawful act, <OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant did not know the wrongfulness of the unlawful act.

##### Irresistible Impulse Test
- Under the _Irresistible Impulse Test_, a defendant is entitled to acquital because of legal insanity if:
	1. [ ] the defendant lacked the capacity for self-control and free choice, <OR/>
	2. [ ] the defendant lacked the capacity to conform their conduct to the law's requirements.

##### Model Penal Code Test
- Under the _Model Penal Code Test_, a defendant is entitled to acquital because of legal insanity if:
	1. [ ] because of a __mental illness__,
	2. [ ] the defendant did not have __substantial capacity__ to:
		- [ ] appreciate the wrongfullness of the unlawful act, <OR/>
		- [ ] conform their conduct to the law's requirements

##### Durham Test
- Under the _Durham Test_, a defendant is entitled to acquittal if:
	1. [ ] the unlawful activity would not have been committed,
	2. [ ] but for the defendant's mental illness

## Legal Excuse: Justification Defenses
Even if the State can prove all the elements of an offense, the defendant may have a _legal excuse_ or _justification defense_.

### Self-defense
- Self-defense is a valid defense against crimes of force.
- Commonly, such crimes include [battery](#battery) and [homicide](#criminal-homicide).
- When assessing a claim of self-defense, courts consider the following factors:
	1. Whether the defendant used [nondeadly force](#self-defense-nondeadly-force),
	2. Whether the defendant used [deadly force](#self-defense-deadly-force),
	3. Whether the defendant owed a [duty to retreat](#duty-to-retreat),
	4. Whether the self-defense is an [imperfect self-defense](#imperfect-self-defense)
	5. Whether the act was done in [defense of others](#defense-of-others)

#### Self-defense: Nondeadly Force
- A person may use nondeadly force in self-defense if:
	1. [ ] the person is without fault,
	2. [ ] the person was confronted with unlawful force, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the person reasonably believed it was necessary to
	4. [ ] protect themselves from
	5. [ ] __imminent use of unlawful force upon them__

#### Self-defense: Deadly Force
- A person may use deadly force in self-defense if:
	1. [ ] the person is without fault,
	2. [ ] the person was confronted with unlawful force, <AND/>
	3. [ ] the person reasonably believed it was necessary to
	4. [ ] protect themselves from
	5. [ ] __imminent death or great bodily harm__

#### Duty to Retreat
- In a minority of jurisdictions, persons have a duty to retreat.
- A defendant may use deadly force only if:
	1. [ ] the attack occurred in the defendant's own home;
	2. [ ] the attack occurred while the defendant was making a lawful arrest; <OR/>
	4. [ ] the assailant was in the process of robbing the defendant
  - ELSE
    - the defendant must retreat if they can safely do so

#### Imperfect Self-defense
- An imperfect self-defense occurs when:
	1. [ ] the defendant killed another,
	2. [ ] based on an honest but unreasonable belief that
	3. [ ] the use of deadly force was necessary to
	4. [ ] prevent death or serious bodily harm
- An imperfect self-defense does not result in a defendant's acquital.
- It can, however, reduce a murder charge to [voluntary manslaughter](#voluntary-manslaughter-heat-of-passion).

#### Defense of Others
- A defendant may defend others with force if:
	1. [ ] they reasonably believe that
	2. [ ] the person assisted has the legal right to
	3. [ ] use force in their own defense
- Note that most jurisdictions do not recognize "imperfect defense of others". I.e., the defendant used deadly force to defend another based on an honest but unreasonable belief that the use of deadly force was necessary assist the other.

### Duress
- A defendant has a valid defense of duress if:
	1. [ ] the defendant reasonably believed that
	2. [ ] another person would imminently inflict
	3. [ ] death or great bodily harm upon
		- [ ] the defendant, or
		- [ ] a family member of the defendant
	4. [ ] if the defendant did not commit the crime

### Mistake of Fact
- A defendant has a valid defense of mistake of fact if:
	1. [ ] the defendant lacked the state of mind
	2. [ ] required for the crime
- If the charged offense is a specific intent crime, the mistake does not have to be reasonable.
- For malice and general intent crimes, the mistake must be reasonable.
- Mistake of fact is irrelevant for strict liability crimes.

## Criminal Negligence
- Criminal negligence is an element of various crimes:
  - [Involuntary Manslaughter](#involuntary-manslaughter)
- For the State to prove criminal negligence, it must demonstrate that:
	1. the defendant's act was one of the following:
			- [ ] a grossly negligent action, or
			- [ ] a failure to act when there was a duty to act,
	<AND/>
	2. [ ] the defendant's act or inaction put the victim at a significant risk of:
			- [ ] seriously bodily harm, or
			- [ ] death
