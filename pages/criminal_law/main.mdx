# Criminal Law

## Essential Elements of a Crime
- Almost all crimes require proof of the following four core elements:
	1. [ ] __Actus Reus__. A physical act.
	2. [ ] __Mens Rea__. A mental state.
	3. [ ] __Causation__. A causal connection between the defendant's physical act or failure to act and the harmful result.
	4. [ ] __Concurrence__. The required mental state must have existed at the time of the crime.
- Mnemonic: ACMENCACO - ACt, MENtal state, CAusation, COncurrence.

## Mental State Requirements
- Under common law, there are four different requisite mental states, depending on the crime:

### Specific Intent
- Crimes that require specific intent include:
	- [ ] solicitation - intent to have the person solicited commit a crime 
	- [ ] attempt - intent to complete a crime
	- [ ] conspiracy - intent to have the crime completed
	- [ ] first-degre murder - premediated intent to kill
	- [ ] assault - intent to commit a battery
	- [ ] larceny and robbery - intent to permanently deprive another of their interest in the property taken
	- [ ] burglary - intent to commit a felony in the dwelling
	- [ ] forgery - intent defraud
	- [ ] false pretenses - intent to defraud
	- [ ] embezzlement - intent to defraud
- For the crimes above, the State must prove that the defendant had an objective to commit the crime.
- The existence of a specific intent cannot be concluded purely from the defendant's acts comprising the crime.
	- But, the way the crime was carried out can provide evidence of intent.
	- e.g., Beating someone with a bat in a fit of rage is very different from someone luring a person to a location and then beating them with a bat.

### Malice Aforethought
- Crimes that require __malice aforethought__ include:
	- [ ] common law murder, and
	- [ ] arson
- To demonstrate malice, the State must prove that:
	- [ ] the defendant had a reckless disregard of an obvious or high risk that the particular harmful result will occur.
- Defenses to specific intent crimes (e.g., voluntary intoxication) do not apply to malice crimes. 

### General Intent
- General intent is a catch-all category of intent.
- To demonstrate general intent, the State must prove that:
	- [ ] the defendant intended to commit an act
	- [ ] prohibited by law
- Note: Whether the defendant intended the act's result is irrelevant.
- General intent crimes include:
	- [ ] battery,
	- [ ] rape,
	- [ ] manslaughter,
	- [ ] kidnapping, and
	- [ ] false imprisonment

### Strict Liability
- Strict liability crimes are crimes that only require the state to show:
	- [ ] the defendant voluntarily committed the crime
- Defenses that negate a state of mind do not apply.
- With strict liability crimes, the defendant's actual intent is irrelevant.
- All that matters is that they voluntarily committed the acts comprising the crime.
- Strict liability crimes include:
	- [ ] statutory rape,
	- [ ] selling liquor to minors, and
	- [ ] polygamy
