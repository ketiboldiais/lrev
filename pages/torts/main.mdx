## Intentional Torts

- To establish a prima facie case for an intentioanl tort, the plaintiff must prove the following:
  1. [ ] **Voluntary Act.** The defendant's actions must be voluntary (e.g., not a reflex).
  2. [ ] **Intent**. The defendant must have intent through one of the
         following:
     - [ ] **Specific Intent**. An actor has specific intent when they act with
           the purpose of causing the consequence.
     - [ ] **General Intent**. An actor has general intent when the actor knows
           that the consequence is substantially certain to occur. - NUANCE:
           Transferred Intent Doctrine.
  3. [ ] **Causation**. Causation is satisfied if the defendant's conduct was a
         substantial factor in bringing about the harm.

## Transferred Intent Doctrine

- An actor does not have to intend the specific injury that results from their
  actions to be liable for an intentional tort.
- Example: ${A}$ swings a bat at ${B,}$ breaking ${B}$'s arm. It's not necessary
  for ${A}$ to have intended to break ${B}$'s arm.
- Under the transferred intent doctrine, a defendant can be held liable if they
  intend to commit an intentional tort against one person but instead commit:
  1. A different intentional tort,
  2. The same intentional tort against a different person, or
  3. A different intentional tort against a different person.
- The transferred intent doctrine applies to the intentional torts of:
  1. assault,
  2. battery,
  3. false imprisonment,
  4. trespass to land, and
  5. trespass to chattels.

## Battery

- A battery occurs when the defendant:
  1. [ ] causes or is a substantial factor in bringing about,
  2. [ ] harmful or offensive contact
     - Harmful contact := contact that causes injury, pain, or illness.
     - Offensive contact := contact that a person ordinary sensibility would
       find offensive (e.g., spitting on someone)
  3. [ ] to the plaintiff's person
     - "to the plaintiff's person" includes anything connected to the plaintiff
       (e.g., pulling a chair beneath some)
  4. [ ] has specific or general intent
- Note that unlike assault, battery has no requirement for apprehension.

## Assault

- An assault occurs when the defendant:
  1. [ ] causes or is a substantial factor in bringing about,
  2. [ ] reasonable apprehension in the plaintiff
     - This means that bodily contact is not required for an assault, only a
       reasonable apprehension of such contact.
     - the plaintiff must be aware of the defendant's actions to have
       reasonable apprehension (i.e., this requirement isn't met if the
       plaintiff is unconscious)
     - Generally, mere words do not constitute assault
  3. [ ] of imminent harmful or offensive bodily contact to the plaintiff's
         person,
     - the plaintiff must be apprehensive that they're about to become the
       victim of an immedate battery.
     - there can't be a significant delay (i.e., threats of future harm don't
       count)
  4. [ ] has specific or general intent

## False Imprisonment

- A false imprisonment occurs when the defendant:
  1. [ ] Causes or is a substantial factor in bringing about,
     - the defendant may cause confinement of the plaintiff through the use of
       physical barriers, force, threats, invalid use of authority, duress, or
       failure to provide a safe means of escape
  2. [ ] the plaintiff's confinement within fixed boundaries, and
     - confinement within fixed boundaries exists when the plaintff's movement
       is limited in all directions, such that there is no reasonable means of
       escape known to the plaintiff
  3. [ ] has specific or general intent
     - the plaintiff must be aware of the confinement or be harmed by it

### Shopkeeper's Privilege

- A shopkeeper can detain a suspected shoplifter as long as the detainment is
  reasonable in both time and manner.

## Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

- Four requirements for an IIED. The defendant:
  1. [ ] acted with extreme or outrageous conduct
     - "extreme or outrageous" := conduct that a reasoable person would regard
       as intolerable in a civilized society
  2. [ ] caused or was a substantial factor in bringing about
  3. [ ] severe and emotional distress, and
  4. [ ] had intent to cause several emotional distress or acts with
         recklessness as to the risk of causing severe emotional distress
     - Reckless conduct satisfies the intent requirement.
     - BUT the transferred intent doctrine does not apply.

## Harms to Property Interests

### Trespass to Land

- 3 core requirements for trespass to land:
  1. [ ] the defendant caused or is a substantial factor in bringing about
  2. [ ] a physical invasion of the plaintiff's real property, **and**
     - the invasion may be by person (e.g., walking onto the year)
     - the invasion may be by object (e.g., dumping trash on the land)
  3. [ ] has specific or general intent
- Notice that there is no requirement for damage to property. It doesn't matter if the property is unscathed.

### Trespass to Chattels

- 3 core requirements for trespass to chattels:
  1. [ ] the defendant caused or is a substantial factor in bringing about
  2. [ ] an interference with the plaintiff's right of possession in chattel,
         **and**
     - there are two forms of interference:
       1. ~interference by intermeddling~ := when the defendant directly
          damages the chattel
       2. ~interfence by dispossession~ := when the defendant deprives the
          plaintiff of their lawful right of possession of the chatel
  3. [ ] has specific or general intent

### Conversion

- 4 core requirements for convensions:
  1. [ ] causes or is a substantial factor in bringing about
  2. [ ] an interference with the plaintiff's right of possession in chatel
  3. [ ] where the interference is so serious, it deprives the plaintiff
         entirely of the use of the chattel, and
     - unlike trespass to chatells, the interference must be serious
     - if liable, the defendant must pay the chattel's full value
  4. [ ] has specific or general intent

### Landlords & Tenants
- As long as a subleasee is in legal possession of the apartment, the subleasee has standing to sue their landlords for trespass.
- Example:
> - ${P}$ rents an apartment from landlord ${D.}$
> - ${D}$ is homophobic.
> - After finding out ${P}$ is gay, ${D}$ begins harassing ${P}$:
>   - ${D}$ enters the apartment unannounced and without consent,
>   - ${D}$ removes the handles from all the faucets.
- ${P}$ can sue ${D}$ for trespass.
- ${P}$ can collect compensatory damages (for emotional distress and removal of the faucet handles).
- ${P}$ can collect punitive damages (based on ${D}$'s malicious intent and ill will).
- If the lease is still in effect and the trespasses are repeated and ongoing, ${P}$ can pursue injunctive relief.

## Defenses to Intentional Torts

The following are common defenses to intentional torts.

### Consent

There is no intentional tort if:

1. [ ] the plaintiff consented to the defendant's conduct,
2. [ ] the plaintiff's consent was valid, **and**
3. [ ] the plaintiff's consent is express or implied through words or conduct

- Consent can be found invalid if:
  - consent was obtained through fraud,
  - the plaintiff lacked capacity to provide consent

### Self-defense & Defense of Others

There is no intentional tort if:

1. [ ] the defendant reasonably believed that the plaintiff was going to harm
       the defendant or another, and
2. [ ] the defendant used only the amount of force reasonably ncessary and
       proportionate to protect himself or another

Some nuances to this defense:

- A reasonable mistake as to the existence of danger to the defendant or the
  person the defendant is attempt to protect is allowed.
- The defendant is not permitted to claim self-defense if the defendant was the
  initial aggressor, unless the other party responded to non-deadly force with
  deadly force.
- A defendant may use reasonable reasonable force if they believe it's
  reasonably necessary to prevent tortious harm to their property.
  - BUT: deadly force cannot be used to prevent harm to property.

### Necessity

- Necessary is a defense against trespass to land and trespass to chattels.
- There is no intentional tort if:
  1. [ ] the defendant:
     1. [ ] entered onto the plaintiff's land, or
     2. [ ] interfered with the plaintiff's personal property, **and**
  2. [ ] the defendant did so to prevent
     1. [ ] an injury, or
     2. [ ] some other severe harm
- If the defendant succeeds on a necessity defense, there are two possible
  outcomes:
  1. ~private necessity~
     - If the defendant's actions:
       - [ ] benefited a limited number of people
       - then the defense is a _private necessity defense_.
     - the defendant must pay for the actual damages caused.
     - the landowner **cannot** use force to exclude the defendant.
  2. ~public necessity~
     - If the defendant's actions:
       - [ ] were done for the public good
       - then the defense is a **public necessity defense**.
     - the defendant is _not_ liable for the public good.

## Negligence
- There are four prima facie requirements for a negligence claim.
  1. [ ] the defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff to follow a specific
         standard of care
  2. [ ] the defendant breached that duty
  3. [ ] the breach was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's
         injuries, and
  4. [ ] the plaintiff sustained actual damages or loss
- A helpful mnemonic: "DuBreCaD" — duty, breach, cause, and damages.
- Violation of an explicit statute establishes a prima facie case of negligence.
- But, the defendant can always rebut with an alternative explanation for why the event occurred.
  - e.g., an unforeseeable heart attack causes the defendant an inability to comply with the duty or statute.

### Duty

- A duty of care is owed to all foreseeable plaintiffs that may be harmed by the
  defendnat's breach of the applicable standard of care.
- What's the definition for a "foreseeable plaintiff"? Two interpretations:
  1. majority view (Cardozo): the defendant is only liable to plaintiffs within
     the forseaable zone of danger
  2. minority view (Andrews): the defendnat owes a duty to everyone harmed.

### Affirmative Duty to Act

- By default, there is **no** affirmative duty to act affirmatively or help
  others.
- BUT: an affirmative duty to act kicks in if one of the following occurs:
  1. [ ] the defendant places the plaintiff in danger,
  2. [ ] the defendant has a special relationship with the plaintiff,
  3. [ ] the defendant has an affirmative duty by law, or
  4. [ ] the defendant begins to administer aid or attempt to rescue the
         plaintiff
     - if a person chooses to rescue or aid another, they are liable for the
       injuries caused by their failure to exercise reasonable care in
       performing that aid or rescue

### Standards of Care

There are several standards of care applicable in negligence claims.

#### The Reasonably Prudent Person Standard (RPPS)

- the default standard of care owed by a defendant to a plaintiff is the
  **reasonably prudent person standard**. If none of the special standards that
  follow apply, RPPS applies.
  > Objectively, what would a reasonably prudent person do under the
  > circumstances?
- factors
  1. ~Physical Disabilities~
     - physical disabilities are considered (e.g., blindness, deafness,
       muteness).
     - thus, if the defendant has no legs, the standard of care is that of a
       reasonably prudent paraplegic under the circumstances.
  2. ~Intoxication~
     - Intoxicated people are held to the same standard as sober people.
     - UNLESS: the intoxication was involuntary (e.g., someone spiked the
       defendant's drink).
  3. ~Community Customs~
     - Community customs may be relevant in determining reasonableness, but
       they aren't dispositive.

#### Reasonably Prudent Child Standard

- If the defendant's a child, the question to ask is:
  > Objectively, what would a reasonably prudent child of similar age,
  > experience, and intelligence under the circumstances do?
- EXCEPTION:
  - If the child is engaged in adult activity (e.g., operating a crane), the
    court will not take the child's age into account — the child will be held to
    an adult standard.

#### Professional Standards

- Professionals include: nurses, lawyers, accountants, engineers, architects,
  etc.
- The question to ask:
  > What would a member of that profession in good standing in similar
  > communities do?

##### Physicians

- Physicians are held to a national standard of care and have a duty to disclose
  the risks of treatment to enable a patient to give informed consent.
- Physicians breach this duty ONLY IF:
  - [ ] the physician did not disclose the risk,
  - [ ] the risk was so serious that a reasonable person would not have
        consented if they knew of the risk

##### Psychotherapists
- majority of states: psychotherapists have a duty to warm potential victims of
  a patient's serious threats of harm if:
  - [ ] the patient has:
    - [ ] apparent intent, and
    - [ ] ability
  - [ ] to carry out such threats, and
  - [ ] the potential victim is readily identifiable

#### Landowners
- traditional approach: the standard of care owed by landowners to an entrant
  depends on the entrant's status
1. ~Trespassers~
    - trespasser := a person who enters a landowner's property without valid
      consent or necessity. There are two types of trespassers:
      1. ~discovered/anticipated trespassers~
        - these are trespassers who enter the land without consent, but may
          be expected by the landowner.
        - the landowner owes a duty to:
          - [ ] warn of (or make safe) hidden dangers on the land that
          - [ ] pose a risk of death or serious bodily harm
        - note: this only applies to artificial conditions that the
          landowner is aware of
      2. ~undiscovered trespassers~
        - trespassers that enter the land without consent and are _not_
          expected by the landowner.
        - the landowner owes no duty to undiscovered trespassers.
2. ~licensees~
    - licensee := a person who lawfully enters the landowner's property for
      her own purpose or benefit rather than for the landowner's benefit.
      - e.g., social guests or appraisers
    - the landowner has NO duty to inspect their property for licensees
    - BUT: the landowner owes a duty to:
      1. [ ] warn of, or make safe
      2. [ ] hidden dangers on the land that pose an unreasonable risk of
            harm
        - this applies to both artificial and natural conditions that the
          landowner is aware of
3. ~invitees~
    - invitee := a person invited on the property for the landowner's own
      benefit or mutual benefit with the person
      - e.g., a customer shopping in a store
    - the landowner owes a duty to:
      1. [ ] reasonably inspect the land for hidden dangers (artifical or
            natural)
      2. [ ] that pose an unreasonable risk of harm,
      3. [ ] if such dangers are discovered, the landowner must make them
            safe
        - e.g., installing a warning sign, drying the wet floor, etc
- NOTE: Some states do not recognize the distinction between licensees and
  invitees, and instead apply a reasonable person standard to all landowners.
- i.e., the landowner owes a duty of reasonable care to _all_ entrants.

- In general, landowners cannot use deadly force to protect property.
- The exception depends on two factors
  1. whether the state:
    1. imposes on landowners a duty to retreat, or
    2. grants landowners a right to stand their ground, <AND/>
  2. the extent of the **curtilage** (the area surrounding the landowner's physical home) that counts as the landowner's dwelling

##### Stand-your-ground States
- States with stand-your-ground laws allow people to use force if necessary when confronted with a threat of harm. In the map below, these states are colored blue.
- Some states allow people to use such force only if they're inside their vehicles. In the map, these states are colored yellow.
- Other states do not provide explicit stand-your-ground laws, but use stand-your-ground in practice (e.g., through jury instructions or case law). These states are colored purple in the map.
##### Castle Doctrine States
- States that follow the castle doctrine allow people to defend themselves using force while in their home or their vehicles, but must retreat in public places. In the map below, these states are colored red.
##### Duty to Retreat States
- The lone state with a duty to retreat is Hawaii. Use of deadly force is permitted only after the person has retreated.
##### Fact-dependent States
- Two jurisdictions are fact-dependent: Vermont and DC.
- DC has no stand-your-ground laws, and case law allows juries to consider a failure to retreat.
- Vermont does not have stand-your-ground laws, nor does it follow the castle doctrine. Case law, however, has made it clear there is no duty to retreat if a person is attacked in their dwelling.

##### Artifical Conditions
- Landowners have a duty to keep their premises free of dangerous conditions <IFF/>:
  - [ ] the landowner knows, or has reason to know,
  - [ ] that trespassers are in dangerous proximity to the condition, <AND/>
  - [ ] the trespassers are unlikely to appreciate the risk.
- Even if the landowner has a such a duty, the landowner only has a duty to:
  - exercise reasonable care to warn trespassers of the danger.

##### Attractive Nuisance
Attractive nuisances are artificial conditions that children are likely to play with or in. E.g., a large tree that turns out to be poisonous. Generally, landowners have a duty to warn (e.g., posting a sign saying the tree is poisonous), indicating the presence of danger or condition.

That duty, however, exists only if the landowner has reason to know that children are likely to trespass. If the tree was located in a preserve hundreds of miles away from the nearest neighborhood, the landowner has no reason to know that children likely to trespass.

- Given a landowner ${L,}$ if the following conditions are met:
  - [ ] ${L}$ knows, or has reason to know,
  - [ ] an artificial condition on their land
  - [ ] exists in a place where
    - [ ] children are likely to trespass, and
  - [ ] the landowner knows or reason to know that the artifical condition poses
        an unreasonable risk of death or serious bodily harm, and
  - [ ] the children do not appreciate the danger involved, and
  - [ ] the risk of harm outweighs the expense of making the condition safe,
- then ${L}$ owes a duty to warn child trespassers of the artificial conditions
  on the land

#### Daycares
A common lawsuit these days is with daycares. Suppose the following:

> ${A}$, a child, is enrolled in daycare ${B}$. ${B}$ is located near a lake. While under ${B}$'s care, ${A}$ goes off into the woods, enters the lake, and drowns. ${A}$'s parents file suit against ${B.}$

1. Daycares are not strictly or absolutely liable for all injuries that occur to children in their care. If ${A}$'s parents file a negligence claim, they must prove all the elements of negligence — duty, breach, cause, and damages.
2. The fact that the daycare was near a lake — or some other potentially dangerous condition — is not in itself evidence of negligence.  
    - But, it could mean that the standard of reasonable care requires more thorough supervision.
3. Ultimately, for the parents to prevail, they must prove that the daycare did not exercise reasonable care. E.g., the employee overseeing ${A}$ was asleep, on their phone the entire time, drunk, etc.

#### Negligence Per Se
- some states have statutes that impose specific duties for the benefit or
  protection of others.
- a violation of this statute constitutes ~negligence per se~.
- a defendant is liable under negligence per se if their violation of the
  statute was the proximate cause of the plaintiff's injury.

#### Contributory Negligence

- In a contributory negligence jurisdiction, 𝑝 **cannot** recover damages if their negligence contributed to their injury in any way.
- EXCEPTION: 𝑝 can recover if:
  1. [ ] 𝑑 engaged in _wanton and willful misconduct_, OR
  2. [ ] 𝑑 had the _last clear chance_ to avoid injury the plaintiff, but failed to do so.
- contributory negligence is **NOT** a defense for **intentional torts**.

#### Comparative Negligence

- For ~comparative fault jurisdictions~:
  - If 𝑝 was negligent themselves => recovery is limited
    - but, this does not necessarily bar recovery.

##### Pure Comparative Negligence

- Under pure comparative fault, 𝑝's recover is limited by the percentage of fault the jury attributes to 𝑝's own negligence.
- E.g., if the jury finds 𝑝 is 95% at fault, 𝑝 can only recover 5% of their damages.

##### Modified Comparative Negligence

- Under modified comparative negligence, 𝑝's recover is limited by the percentage of fault the jury attributes to 𝑝's own negligence.
- However, if 𝑝 is more at fault than 𝑑, then 𝑝's recovery is completely barred.
- Example:
  - If the jury finds 𝑝 is 51% at fault, 𝑝 cannot recover damages.
  - If the jury finds 𝑝 is 50% at fault, 𝑝 can recover 50% of their damages.
    - NOTE: for some jurisdictions, if 𝑝 and 𝑑 are equally at fault, 𝑝 cannot recover.
  - If the jury finds 𝑝 is 10% at fault, 𝑝 can recover 90% of their damages.

#### Assumption of Risk

- this defense applies iff:
  - 𝑝 _knowingly and willingly_ embraces a risk for some purpose of their own.
- assumption of risk can be express or implied.

## Proof of Fault

- proof of fault will usually be established by evidence.
- however, plaintiffs can also use the doctrine of ~res ipsa loquitur~.

### Res Ipsa Loquitur

- Res ipsa loquitur is applied when an element of negligence is difficult to
  prove, but the circumstances make it obvious that the defendant's negligence
  was the most likely cause of the harm.
- for res ipsa loquitur to apply, the plaintiff must show:
  - [ ] that the accident resulting in the harm was:
    1. [ ] of a kind that ordinarily does not occur in the absence of
           negligence, and
    2. [ ] caused by an agent or instrumentality within the defendant's
           exclusive control
- some states also require that the plaintiff prove that the injury was not due
  to any action on their part

## Ordinary Contacts in Daily Life

- People are presumed to consent to the ordinary contacts of daily life.
- There is no negligence for ordinary contacts in daily life.
- E.g., tapping someone on the shoulder to get their attention.
- <UNLESS />- The plaintiff indicates that they do not want such contacts. - E.g, the
  plaintiff's caretake says, "Please do not shake the plaintiff's hand."

### Actual Cause

- There are two ways to prove actual cause:

     - The plaintiff must show:
       - [ ] if it weren't for the defendant's breach, the injury would not

  2. ~Substantial Factor Test~
     - the plaintiff must show that:
       - [ ] the defendant's actions were a substantial factor in causing the
             plaintiff's injury

### Proximate Cause

- to prove proximate cause, the plaintiff must show that her injury was:
  - [ ] a **foreseeable result** of the defendant's conduct

#### Defense: Intervening Cause

> ${A}$ crashes into ${B.}$ Moments later, lightning strikes ${A,}$ causing him severe burns. The lightning is considered an _intervening cause_, and it's _unforeseeable_. ${B}$'s liability is limited to only the injuries that occurred before the lightning, if any. This excludes ${A}$'s liability for ${A}$'s burns.

- if:
  - [ ] an outside force or action contributes to the plaintiff's harm
  - [ ] **after** the defendant's breach occurs, and
  - [ ] the force or action is unforseeable
- then
  => the defendant's liability to the plaintiff is cut off from that point forward
- pushback:
  - further forseeable acts are forseeable (e.g., medical malpractice)
  - BUT:
    - criminal acts, intentional torts, and nature induced 'acts of god' are considered unforseeable
- Most courts will find most of these injuries foreseeable:
  - injuries sustained when running from danger,
  - injuries sustained to the rescuer during a rescue attempt
- But, the ~fireman's rule~ pushes back on this:

#### Fireman's Rule

- police officers and firefighters are barred from collecting damages that occur in the course of their duties, even if the injuries were a clear result of the other party's negligence.

- The fireman's rule only bars claims that are unique or special to the plaintiff's inherently dangerous work.

### Eggshell Plaintiff Rule

- The ~eggshell plaintiff rule~ provides:
  - the defendant is liable for **all harm** suffered by the plaintiff,
  - EVEN IF the plaintiff suffered from an unforeseeable, preexisting mental or physical condition that aggravates the harm.

## Vicarious Liability

> `A` works for `EFG Construction Corp.`. While operating a truck for `EFG`, `A` backs into `B`, breaking his leg. Is `EFG` liable?

### Respondeat Superior

- Under the doctrine of ~respondeat superior~, an employer (principal) may be liable for torts committed by an employee (agent) if:
  1. [ ] [employer-relationship exists](#existence-of-employee-employer-relationship), and
  2. [ ] the employee's commission of the torth occurs within the [scope of employment](#scope-of-employment).

#### Existence of Employee-employer Relationship

- To determine if an employee-employer relationship exists, the most important consideration is:
  - _the extent of control that the principal exercises over the details of the agent's work._
- more control => greater likelihood the agent is an employee
- less control => less likelihood the agent is an employee
- independent contracts are not considered employees

#### Scope of Employment

- activity is within the scope of employment when:
  - [ ] the employee's conduct is:
    1. [ ] of the same general nature as that authorized, or
    2. [ ] incidental to the conduct authorized by the employer
- to make the determination above, courts consider the questions:
  1. was the employee's conduct a function for which the employee was hired to perform?
  2. was the employee's conduct within the employer's authorized time and space limits?
  3. was the employee's conduct done to serve the employer?
  4. was the employee's conduct foreseeable to the employer?
- an employer remains liable during an employee's ~detour~ (a minor deviation from the scope of employment).
  - this applies even if the detour is mainly for the employee's own personal reasons.
- DEFENSE: the employer does not remain liable during an employee's ~frolic~ (a major deviation from the scope of employment).

##### Intentional Torts by Employees

- Default rule: Employers are **not** liable for an employee's intentional tort.
- COUNTER:
  - An employer is liable for an employee's intentional tort IF:
    - [ ] the intentional tort was authorized by the employer, OR
    - [ ] use of force is within the scope of employment
      - e.g., security guards

### Business Partners

- The negligence of one busines partner can be ~imputed~ on other business partners if:
  - [ ] the negligent act is committed within the scope of the business's purpose

### NIED: Zone of Danger

- 𝑝 can recover for NIED if:
  1. [ ] 𝑑 negligently caused a threat of physical impact;
  2. [ ] 𝑝 was within the zone of danger of the threatened physical impact, and
  3. [ ] the threat of physical impact caused emotional distress

### NIED: Bystander Recovery

- 𝑝 can recover for NIED if:
  1. [ ] the defendant negligently inflicted bodily injury to another,
  2. [ ] the plaintiff is closely related to the person injured by the defendant,
  3. [ ] the plaintiff was present at the scene of the injury, AND
  4. [ ] the plaintiff personally observed the injury
  5. [ ] some jurisdictions also require:
     - the plaintiff must manifest physical symptoms after witnessing the injury

### NIED: Special Relationship

- 𝑝 can recover for NIED where a pre-existing relationship exists between 𝑑 and 𝑝.
  - e.g., doctor-patient
- common way this arises:
  1. 𝑑 negligently mishandles a corpse, or
  2. 𝑑 provides false medical information
  - e.g., 𝑑, a doctor, tells 𝑝, a patient, that 𝑝 has cancer when she does not.

## Liability of Multiple Defendants

### Joint & Several Liability

- when 2 or more parties are ~jointly and severally liable~, each party is independently liable for the **full extent** of the damages resulting from the tort.
  - => if 𝑝 wins a money judgment against jointly and severally liable defendants, 𝑝 can collect the full value of the judgment from any one of them.
- ~Contribution~ allows 𝑑 who pays **more than their share** of the total liability to recover from the other liable defendants.
  - DEFENSE: For states that follow a ~pure comparative fault theory~, 𝑝 cannot recover more than the other liable 𝑑's percentage share of fault.

### Alternative Liability

- if 𝑝 cannot specifically identify which among multiple 𝑑s caused their harm, ~alternative liability~ allows 𝑝 to shift the burden of proving causation to the defendants.[^alt_liability_note]

[^alt_liability_note]: Even if only one of them could have been responsible.

### Indemnification

- ~indemnification~ arises in situations involving:
  1. vicarious liability, or
  2. strict liability
- indemnification allows a ~passive tortfeasor~ who was forced to pay damages to recover a complete reimbursement from an active tortfeasor.

### Animals & Abnormally Dangerous Activity

#### Domestic Animals

- Domestic animal := an animal that's been trained over time to live and breed in a tame condition (dogs, cats, farm animals, etc).
- Domestical animal owners are strictly liable for harm caused by the animal ONLY IF:
  - [ ] the owner knows the animal is potentially dangerous, or
  - [ ] the owner has reason to know the animal is potentially dangerous

#### Wild Animals

- Wild animal := an animal that, as a species or class, is not customarily kept in the service of mankind (e.g., tigers, monkeys, etc.).
- Wild animal owners **are strictly liable** for harm caused by the animal regardless of safety precautions taken by the owner.
  - This is true even if the harm occured because of a force of nature.
  - E.g., ${P}$ sees a tiger owned by ${D}$ in the wild. ${B}$ runs in fear and falls down a cliff. ${D}$ is liable for ${P}$'s injuries because tigers are wild animals.
- <Red text={"DEFENSE"} />- Wild animal owners are not strictly liable for harm caused to trespassers.

### Abnormally Dangerous Activities

- A defendant is strictly liable for damages caused to the plaintiff if the defendant is engaged in **abnormally dangerous activities**.
- An abnormally dangerous activity is one that's:
  1. [ ] inherently dangerous,
  2. [ ] inappropriate for the location chosen,
  3. [ ] virtually impossible to make safe, and
  4. [ ] of little value to the community

### Defective Products

- for a plaintiff to prevail under products liability, the plaintiff must show all of the following:
  1. [ ] the product was defective in manufacture, design, or failure to warn,
  2. [ ] the defect existed when the product left the defendant's control, and
  3. [ ] the defect caused the plaintiff's injury when the product was used in a foreseeable way.

#### Defective in Manufacture

- to show a manufacturing defect, the plaintiff must show:
  1. [ ] the product deviated from its intended design, and
  2. [ ] the product fails to conform to the manufacturer's own design
- The seller of a product with a manufacturing defeect is strictly liable for the injuries caused by the defect.
  - It's irrelevant that the seller had no part in manufacturing the product.
  - There's no defense that the buyer should have looked at the product more closely, because contributory negligence is not a defense against strict product liability.

##### Professionals Using Faulty Equipment

- Suppose Dr. ${A}$ operates on ${B}$ and their scalpel breaks because of a manufacturing defect. Is ${A}$ liable under products liability?
- No. to be liable under products liability, the defendant must be in the business of selling the product — not merely using.
- The ${A}$ could be liable for malpractice, but only if they deviated from the professional standard of care.

#### Defective in Design

- there are two ways for a plaintiff to show design defects:
  1. <Red text={"Consumer Expectation Test"} />- the plaintiff must show that: - [ ] the
     product is less safe than the ordinary consumer would expect
  2. <Red text={"Risk-utility Test"} />- the plaintiff must show that: - [ ] the product's
     risks outweight its benefits, and - [ ] there is a reasonable alternative design

#### Failure to Warn

- to prove a failure to warn, the plaintiff must show:
  1. [ ] the plaintiff was not warned of the risks regarding the use of the product,
  2. [ ] the risks are not obvious to an ordinary user, and
  3. [ ] the designer/manufacturer was in fact aware of such risks

### Scope of Products Liabilities

#### Who Can Be a Plaintiff?

- Any person foreseeably injured by a defective product can pursue a products liability claim.
  - this includes: purchasers, bystandards, other users, etc.

#### Who Can Be a Defendant?

- A strict liability claim under products liability can only be brought against a ~merchant~.
- For purposes of products liability, am ~merchant~ is:
  1. [ ] one who routinely deals in goods of the type, and
  2. [ ] is in the product's chain of distribution
  - e.g., designer => manufacturer => wholesaler => retailer

## Defamation

- A defamation occurs when the defendant:
  1. [ ] published
  2. [ ] a defamatory statement
  3. [ ] of or concerning the plaintiff
  4. [ ] causing damage to the plaintiff's reputation

### Publication

- to satisfy the "defendant published" requirement, the following conditions must be met:
  1. [ ] the statement must be communicated to a third party, and
  2. [ ] the third party must understand the content of the statement
- NOTE: a person who repeats a defamatory statement may still be held liable for defamation

### Defamatory Statement

- to satisfy the "defamatory statement" requirement, the following conditions must be met:
  1. [ ] the statement must be false, and
  2. [ ] the statement must not be an opinion
- defamatory statements usually contain language that diminishes respect to the plaintiff or deters others from associating with the plaintiff.

### Plantiff Targeting

- to satisfy the "of or concerning the plaintiff" requirement, the following condition must be met:
  1. [ ] if a reasonable person heard the statement, the person would believe the defamatory statement referred to the plaintiff

### Standard of Review

- the standard required to prove defamation depends on whether the plaintiff is a public or private individual.

#### Public Officials

- a ~public official~ is a person who has control over government office.
  - this includes political candidates.
- for public officials, plaintiffs must prove **actual malice** by showing:
  1. [ ] the person who made the defamatory statement knew it was false, OR
  2. [ ] the person who made the defamatory statement acted with reckless regard for the truth

#### Public Figures

- there are two kinds of public figures:
  1. ~general purpose public figures~
     - a _general purpose public figure_ is a person of persuasive power and influence in society
  2. ~limited purpose public figures~
     - a _limited purpose public figure_ is a person who has injected themselves into a public contrversy

#### Private Individuals

- a private individual is any person that is not a public official or public figure.
- IF:
  1. [ ] the plaintiff is a private individual, AND
  2. [ ] the defamatory statement is a matter of public concern,
  - THEN: the plaintiff only has to prove that:
    1. the statement was false, AND
    2. the speaker was at least negligent

### Limitations on Damages

- IF:
  - [ ] the plaintiff is a public official, OR
  - [ ] the plaintiff is a public figure,
        => then the plaintiff can only recover actual proven damages
    - this excludes punitive or presumed damages
- IF:
  1. [ ] the plaintiff is a private individual,
  2. [ ] the statement made is a matter of public concern, AND
  3. [ ] the plaintiff can show actual malice,
  - => the plaintiff can recover actual damages, as well as punitive or presumed damages
  - => If the plaintiff fails cond (3), then the plaintiff can only recover actual damages.

#### Libel vs. Slander

- ~Libel~ := a written, printed, or record statement.
- ~Slander~ := a spoken statement that is not recorded.
- If the defamatory statement is slander, then the plaintiff can only recover special damages.
  - this requires a more concrete showing of actual economic loss.
- however, if ~slander per se~ applies, the plaintiff is not limited to special damages.
- there are four slander per se categories in common law:
  1. statements that impugn the plaintiff's business integrity or skill,
  2. statements of unchastity about an unmarried woman,
  3. statements of the plaintiff having a loathsome disease, and
  4. statements attributing a crime of moral turpitude to the plaintiff

### Libel

- For common law libel claims, it doesn't matter whether the speaker investigated if the statement was true before spreading it.
- If the plaintiff authorizes the speaker to investigate the rumor, they consent to a limited publication in response to inquiries.
- Damages to reputation from an alleged libel are presumed.

## Damage Allocation

Suppose the following:
- ${A}$, ${B}$, and ${C}$ are found liable for a tort committed against ${D.}$
- The total damages is $100,000.
- Their fault percentages are as follows:

| ${A}$ | ${B}$ | ${C}$ | ${D}$ |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 20%   | 50%   | 20%   | 10%   |

What is the maximum amount ${D}$ can recover from ${B}$?
- Under ~joint and several liability~:
  - the entire amount can be collected from any one of the defendants.
  - if a single defendant must pay, that defendant can seek to recover a proportional share of the damages from the other defendants.
  - Example:
    - ${D}$ gets to collect all \$100,000. It doesn't matter who pays — as long as ${D}$ gets the \$100,000.
    - Suppose ${B}$ pays the the entire \$100,000.
      - Note: ${B}$ only owed \$50,000. Thus, ${B}$ can go after ${A}$ and ${C}$ for the \$50,000 extra they paid.
- Under ~several liability~:
  - the amount the plaintiff collected is the amount the particulat defendant is found liable for.
  - Example:
    - Under several liability, ${D}$ can only recover \$50,000 from ${B}$.
    - Why? Because ${B}$ is only severally liable for 50% of the damages — \$50,000.
- Under ~contributory negligence~:
  - ${D}$ collects nothing.
  - Why? Because they're 10% at fault.
