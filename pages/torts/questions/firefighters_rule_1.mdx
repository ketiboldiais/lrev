<Quiz title="Fireman's Rule 1">
- Driver returns home from a long work shift.
- Driver falls asleep at the wheel and loses control of their car.
- Driver collides with a police car driven by a cop returning to the station after responding to an emergency.
- Cop is injured in the accident.
- Cop sues the driver in negligence for her injuries.
- Driver moves for summary judgment. Argument:
	- The common law firefighters’ rule bars the suit.
- Should the court grant the motion?

1. No, because the firefighters’ rule does not apply to police officers.
	- Incorrect.
	- Correct: The driver’s motion should be denied.
	- Wrong: Legal basis for this conclusion.
	- The firefighters’ rule also covers police officers.
	- The rule applies to public servants at risk of injury by the perils that they have been employed to confront.
	- The motion should be denied because being struck by a car in normal traffic is not one of the special risks inherent to dangerous police work.
	- The firefighters’ rule only bars claims for injuries that result from risks that are _unique or special_ to the plaintiff’s inherently dangerous work.
2. No, because the police officer’s injuries were not related to any special dangers of her job.
	- Correct.
	- Driver could be held liable for their negligence because being struck by a car in normal traffic is not one of the special risks inherent to dangerous police work.
	- The firefighters’ rule bars only claims for injuries that result from risks that are unique or special to the plaintiff’s inherently dangerous work.
3. Yes, because the accident would not have occurred but for the emergency.
	- Incorrect.
	- But-for causation is not sufficient to support the firefighters’ rule defense when the risk that materialized was not one of the unique risks inherent to the officer’s dangerous work.
	- The firefighters’ rule bars only claims for injuries that result from risks that are unique or special to the plaintiff’s inherently dangerous work.
	- The fact that the officer was returning from an emergency when she was struck is just a coincidence.
4. Yes, because the police officer was injured on the job.
	- Incorrect.
	- The firefighters’ rule bars only claims for injuries that result from risks that are unique or special to the plaintiff’s inherently dangerous work.
	- Workers’ compensation, not the common law of torts, is the compensation system for on-the-job injuries.
	- The driver could be held liable for his negligence because being struck by a car in normal traffic is not one of the special risks inherent to dangerous police work.
