* A contract is a promise or set of promises, where each promise is legally
  enforceable.
* **Common Law versus Article 2 Sale of Goods**
  - Contract disputes are governed by two bodies of law:

  - Contracts are usually governed by common law.
  - Contracts for the **sale of goods** are governed by ~Article 2 of the
    Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)~.
  - What happens if the UCC and common law conflict?
    - If the contract involves the sale of goods, the UCC controls.

## What's a sale?

- A ~sale~ is a contract where the title to a good passes from the seller to the
  buyer for a price. <UCC sect={'2-106(1)'}/>.
- This definition implies the following necessary elements:
  1.  [ ] a contract,
  2.  [ ] a good,
  3.  [ ] a seller,
  4.  [ ] a buyer,
  5.  [ ] a price,
  6.  [ ] the seller must have title to the good,

## What's a good?

- A ~good~ is _anything movable_ at the time they're identified as the items to
  be sold under the contract. <UCC sect={'2-105(1)'}/>.
- This covers many tangible things: cars, trucks, horses, trailers, etc.
- **EXCEPTIONS**
  - The key requirement is _movable_.
  - This means the UCC does not cover:
    1.  real estate - land, houses
    2.  services, or - golf club membership, gym membership, contract for
        medical or legal services,
    3.  intangibles, - patents, trademarks, construction contracts, copyrights
  - Goods associated with real estate _may or may not_ be covered by the UCC.
    Examples:
    - minerals, crops, uncut timber, land fixtures.

## Who's a merchant?

- A ~merchant~ is anyone who:
  1.  [ ] regularly deals in goods of the kind sold, or
  2.  [ ] through their occupation,
      - [ ] hold themselves out as having the:
        - [ ] knowledge or skill
        - [ ] peculiar to the practices or goods sold
  - <UCC sect={"2-104(1)"} />
- Almost anyone in business can deemed a merchant.
- **EXCEPTIONS**
  - Some Article 2 provisions require the seller to act in a _mercantile
    capacity_ (acting within the scope of their business, rather than in a
    _personal capacity_) to be considered a merchang.
  - Such provisions include:
    - Article 2's Implied Warranty of Merchantability
  - Example:
    > John sells cars for Junk Corp. On Friday, John buys paper for his office
    > printer. Later, John offers to sell his Jeep to his friend Ken. Per
    > Article 2, John is a merchant with respect to the paper purchase, but not
    > with respect to his care sale.

## Good Faith & Fair Dealing

- Suppose A and B enter into a contract.
- Under the common law and UCC:
  - Both A and B have an obligation to:
    1.  perform their promises in good faith, and
    2.  enforce the contract in good faith.
  - Good faith — under the UCC — is defined as:
    - honesty in fact and observance of reasonable commercial standards.
      <UCC sect={"1-201(20)"} />.
  - Good faith under common law depends on the jurisdiction.
- Common way to violate this duty:
  - One party, through their discretion, deprives the other party of the
    contract's benefits.
- <Standard_of_Review/>: Whether a party violates their duty of good faith and
  fair dealing is a ~question of fact~.

- Contracts can be divided into different types, depending on their attributes.

- ~Express Contracts~
  - Contracts formed by _language_, oral or written.
- ~Implied Contracts~
  - Contracts implied by the circumstances/facts — one party indicates assent
    through their _conduct_.
  - E.g., If ${A}$ goes to the barber and lets ${B}$ cut their hair without
    ${A}$ saying no or resisting, an _implied contract_ forms between ${A}$ and
    ${B.}$
- ~Quasi-contracts~
  - Quasi-contracts are **not** contracts.
  - They're orders made by a court to avoid ~unjust enrichment~ by permitting
    the plaintiff to bring an action in restitution to recover the amount of
    benefit the defendant receives.[^quasi-contract-note].

[^quasi-contract-note]:
    The quasi-contract's relationship to contracts is purely historical.

### Contract Acceptance Types

- Contract types also differ in terms of _how acceptance is made_.
  <FlowChart
    data={` a[label="Contract Formation Types"]; b[label="Bilateral\nContracts"]; c[label="Unilateral\nContracts"]; a--b; a--c; `}
    type="graph"
    width={200}
    height={200}
    nodeShape="plain"
  />
- ~Bilateral Contracts~
  - These are contracts where a promise is exchange for a promise.
  - Example:
    > ${A}$ buys ${x}$ from ${B.}$ In this case, ${A}$ promises to buy ${x}$
    > from ${B,}$ and ${B}$ promises to sell ${x}$ to ${A.}$
- ~Unilateral Contracts~
  - These are contracts where the offeror request ~performance~ rather than a
    promise.
  - One party is the _promisor_ (or _offeror_) and the other is the _promisee_
    (_offeree_).
  - The offeror promises to pay ${x}$ once the offeree has completed the
    offeror's request.
  - Example:
    > ${A}$ promises to pay ${B}$ \$20,000 once ${B}$ ships their merchandise.
    > ${B}$ is not obligated to actually ship the merchandies (since, if they
    > fail to do so, they won't get paid). But if ${B}$ does complete shipping,
    > ${A}$ is obligated to pay ${B.}$
  - Most contracts are unilateral contracts.
  - Under the Restatement Second of Contracts, and UCC Article 2, all contracts
    are _indifferent offers_ — they can be accepted by making a promise or
    beginning performance.
  - <Red text={"Requirements for Formation"} /> Under Article 2 of the UCC where
    a unilateral contract is formed:
  1.  Condition 1
      - [ ] the offeror clearly (unambiguously) indicates that
      - [ ] completion is the only form of acceptance,
  2.  Condition 2
      - [ ] the offer is to the public (e.g., a reward), and
      - [ ] the offer clearly contemplates acceptance

### Contract Validity Types

- Contract types are also divided according to their **validity**.
  <FlowChart
    data={` ranksep=1; a[label="Contract Validity Types"]; b[label="Void\nContracts"]; c[label="Voidable\nContracts"]; d[label="Unenforceable\nContracts"]; a--b; a--c; a--d; `}
    type="graph"
    width={310}
    height={310}
  />
- ~Void Contracts~
  - A void contract is a contract _without any legal effect_ from the very
    beginning.
  - Void contracts cannot be enforced by either party.
  - Examples: Contracts made to commit a crime.
- ~Voidable contracts~
  - These are contracts where the parties can _elect to avoid the contract_.
  - Example: Avoiding a contract because of infancy or mental illness.
- ~Unenforceable Contracts~
  - Contracts that are valid, but cannot be enforced.
  - Unenforceability can result from:
    - expiration of the right to enforce because of a contract provision,
    - the statute of frauds prevents enforcement, or
    - public policy prevents enforcement.

### Mutual Assent

- _Mutual assent_ occurs when the parties reach the "same bargain at the same
  time."
- Common way mutual assent occurs: The parties negotiate, and at some point, one
  party makes a proposal (an _offer_) and the other agrees to it (an
  _acceptance_).
- Courts use an **objective measure** - each party is bound to the **apparent
  intention** they manifested to the other.

#### Promise, Undertaking, or Commitment

- For a communication to be an offer, the offeror must have communicated to the
  offeree one of the following:
  - [ ] a promise,
  - [ ] an undertaking,
  - [ ] an undertaking, or
  - [ ] a commitment
- An invitation to begin preliminary negotiations, doesn't count.
- The offeror must communicate an _intent_ to enter a contract.
- To determine if the offeror had intent, courts consider the following factors.

##### Language

- Language such as "I offer" or "I promise" are not necessary, but they are
  indicative of intent.
- Language with certainty — "I'm asking $400,000 for" may or may not indicate
  intent. Parties can argue that it was just a _feeler_.

##### Surrounding circumstances

- Context matters.
- Communications made as a joke, out of anger, or just to brag are unlikely to
  indicate an offer.
- Pushback: Even if the offeror intended it as a joke, if it could be reasonably
  understood as a serious offer, courts can find a valid offer.

##### Prior practice and the parties' relationships

- Offers are more likely to be found if the offeror and the offeree have a
  history of making contracts with one another.

##### Communication method

- The way an offer is communicated can impact whether a communication is a valid
  offer.
- ~Broad Communications Media~
  - The broader the communicating media (e.g., disseminating the offer through
    the public), the more more likely the communication is just a **solication
    of an offer**.
- ~Advertisements~
  - TV ads, catalogs, newspaper ads, etc., containing price quotes are usually
    interpreted as _invitations for offers_ rather than actual offers.
  - Why not? Public policy. If we construed these as offers, we'd run into the
    problem of _overacceptance_ — too many people accepting the offer.
  - But, if the ad contains a definitive language, it can be considered an
    offer.

##### Industry Custom

- Courts can consider industry custom in determining if a communication counts
  as an offer.
